# 1.2 <i>Yaku</i>, Calls, and the Progression of the Round

Mahjong has always been a game with countless variations in detailed rules, whether in offline play or on online battle platforms. Therefore, it must be acknowledged that the “general rules” referred to in this tutorial are primarily aligned with two platforms: <strong><i>Tenhō</i></strong> ([Official Website](http://tenhou.net/)), which launched in 2006 and is universally recognized for having the highest level of play and the most prestigious <i>dan</i> rankings, and <strong><i>Mahjong Soul</i></strong> ([ZH](https://www.maj-soul.com/), [JA](https://mahjongsoul.com/), and [EN](https://mahjongsoul.yo-star.com/) Official Websites), which launched in 2018, shares basically identical rules with <i>Tenhō</i>, and stands as the most popular online mahjong platform today. After all, apart from these two, other platforms that were once popular have all been lost in time, like tears in rain. Meanwhile, the “competitive rules” are primarily modeled after the highly popular <strong>M.League</strong> ([Official Website](https://m-league.jp/)). Although it incorporates <i>akahai</i> and can be seen as a modern competitive rule set that has compromised with casual local rules, we are, after all, a tutorial for the modern era. As for other rule variations that remain fairly common, we will list them separately in a dedicated section.

## 1.2.1 Scores

In any form of mahjong, if a winning hand fulfills certain specific patterns or special conditions in addition to the basic winning shape, it often receives extra rewards. The colloquial idiom 清一色 ([MSC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Chinese): <i>qīngyīsè</i>), commonly heard in everyday Chinese, refers to a mahjong winning hand comprised entirely of a single suit—a pattern known as <i>chin’itsu</i> in Japanese mahjong, where it also yields a substantial payout. Besides <i>chin’itsu</i>, there are over thirty other specific winning shapes or special conditions in Japanese mahjong, collectively known as <strong><i>yaku</i></strong>[^yaku]. The value of a <i>yaku</i> is measured in <strong><i>han</i></strong>[^han], and multiple <i>yaku</i> achieved simultaneously can combine, adding their <i>han</i> values together. 

> In Japanese, <i>yaku</i> achieved through specific hand patterns are called <i>teyaku</i> (hand-based <i>yaku</i>), while those achieved by meeting special conditions are called <i>jōkyō-yaku</i> (situational <i>yaku</i>). Among the latter, except for <i>menzenchin tsumohō</i> and <i>rīchi</i>, all are based on luck (<i>gūzen-yaku</i>).

[^yaku]: Literally “character; role.”

[^han]: Literally “doubles.”

The number of <i>han</i> determines the value of the hand, which in turn determines the score of the winning player. Each player’s score at the end of the match determines their final rank, and the final rank determines the player's mood. At the start of a match of Japanese mahjong, each player most commonly begins with **25,000 score**. A match consists of multiple rounds (see 1.2.3), and scores are calculated and exchanged at the end of each round. Specifically:

- **Non-dealer win by self-draw** (<i>tsumo</i>): The dealer (East) pays the winner 2× the base score[^kihonten], while the other two non-dealers each pay 1× the base score, totaling 4× the base score for the winner.
- **Non-dealer win by discard** (<i>ron</i>): The player who dealt into the winning hand (<i>hōjū</i>) solely pays the winner 4× the base score.
- **Dealer win by self-draw** (<i>tsumo</i>): The three non-dealers each pay the dealer 2× the base score (totaling 1.5× the payout of a non-dealer win).
- **Dealer win by discard** (<i>ron</i>): The player who dealt into the hand solely pays the dealer 6× the base score (1.5× the payout of a non-dealer win).
- **Exhaustive draw** (<i>ryūkyoku</i>, the round ends with no winner after the wall is drawn): Players who are not in <i>tenpai</i> collectively pay a total penalty score of 3,000 (<i>nōten</i> penalty or <i>nōten bappu</i>), which is then split equally among the players who are in <i>tenpai</i> (to be detailed later).

[^kihonten]: The higher the value of the hand, the higher the “base score” (<i>kihonten</i>). The exact algorithm will be detailed in the section on scoring. Actual payments are rounded up to the nearest hundred, meaning a non-dealer’s payment is not always exactly half of the dealer’s payment.

The scoring of Japanese mahjong, in addition to being heavily influenced by the number of <i>han</i>, also retains the classical <i>fu</i> system. To avoid mental calculations during every hand, players generally memorize a score lookup table, which we will discuss in detail later. For now, let us introduce the chips used in Japanese mahjong to represent a player’s current score—<strong><i>tenbō</i></strong> (“scoring sticks”):

As the game progresses, players estimate the potential value of their hand to calculate the conditions required for a comeback, and declare their score immediately upon winning. Modern Japanese mahjong enforces a one-<i>han</i> minimum constraint (<strong><i>īhan shibari</i></strong>). If you do not have any <i>yaku</i>, even if you have formed “four melds and one pair,” you will find that there is no “zero <i>han</i>” column in the score lookup table. In this case, declaring a win means you cannot state a valid score, which constitutes a foul (<i>chombo</i>). Therefore, in addition to focusing on the winning shape, we must simultaneously keep an eye on the <i>yaku</i> we need to achieve.

## 1.2.2 <i>Chī</i> and <i>Pon</i>

To secure at least one <i>yaku</i>, our strategy can be highly simplified into two directions:

- If you have a good prospect of completing any <i>teyaku</i> (hand-based <i>yaku</i>), you can utilize the calling mechanics introduced in this section to advance your hand.
- If the prospects for any <i>teyaku</i> are unclear, you should refrain from making calls. This state is called **closed hand** (<i>menzenchin</i>[^menzen], or simply <i>menzen</i>). As long as you maintain a closed hand until <i>tenpai</i>, you can utilize the powerful situational <i>yaku</i> known as <i>rīchi</i>, which will be covered in Section 1.3.

[^menzen]: Literally “clear in front of the gate.”

Calling tiles—namely <i>chī</i>, <i>pon</i>, and <i>kan</i>—along with the winning hand shapes, form the core mechanics of all variations of mahjong. In Japanese mahjong, this is collectively referred to as <strong><i>naki</i></strong>[^naki] or <strong><i>fūro</i></strong>[^furo]. It is also colloquially called <i>kui</i>[^kui] or <i>shikake</i>[^shikake]. Since this has been a core mechanic since the ancient origins of the game, we will explain it before introducing the uniquely Japanese <i>rīchi</i> mechanic. Take this hand as an example:

[^naki]: Literally “calling.”

[^furo]: Literally “tile set exposure.”

[^kui]: Literally “eating.”

[^shikake]: Literally “setting up.”

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z</m-tehai><br><small>Spoiler alert: This hand already has one guaranteed <i>yaku</i> from the <i>haku</i> triplet.</small></p>

Here, a two-tile combination that is just one tile away from becoming a sequence, such as <m-pai>7m9m</m-pai> or <m-pai>2s3s</m-pai>, is called a <strong><i>tātsu</i></strong>. The former is an <i>kanchan tātsu</i>, while the latter is a <i>ryanmen tātsu</i>.

> In Japanese, a hand that is just one tile away from <i>tenpai</i>, as in this example, is called <i>īshanten</i>. Following the same logic, a hand two tiles away is <i>ryanshanten</i>, and three tiles away is <i>sanshanten</i>.

### 1.2.2.1 <i>Chī</i>

If the <strong>player to your left</strong> (<i>kamicha</i>) discards a tile that can form a sequence (<i>shuntsu</i>) with a <i>tātsu</i> in your hand—such as an <m-pai>8m</m-pai> for this particular hand—you can immediately declare “<strong><i>chī</i></strong>.” You then reveal the <m-pai>7m9m</m-pai> from your hand and take the left player’s <m-pai>8m</m-pai> to form a sequence:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z　8m-7m9m</m-tehai></p>

The rest of your tiles remain upright and concealed. Normally, you would draw a tile on your turn, but since calling a tile effectively counts as drawing one, you proceed immediately to choose a tile from your hand and discard it. In this position, discarding either <m-pai>2s</m-pai> or <m-pai>3s</m-pai> will put you in <i>tenpai</i>, so you would generally discard <m-pai>2s</m-pai> to keep a good wait shape. Afterward, it becomes the turn of the player to your right (<i>shimoncha</i>) to draw and discard—though they could also choose to call your <m-pai>2s</m-pai>.

Once an open group is formed—including via <i>pon</i> and <i>kan</i>—you can no longer discard tiles from it or swap tiles within it. In Japanese mahjong, open groups like the <m-pai>8m-7m9m</m-pai> shown in the diagram must be placed together at the bottom-right corner of your trick area. Furthermore, the <m-pai>8m</m-pai> must be placed on the left side of the meld and turned sideways to indicate that it came from <i>kamicha</i>. Similarly, <i>pon</i> and <i>kan</i> can be made from any player: if the tile comes from the player opposite you (<i>toimen</i>) or <i>shimoncha</i>, the middle tile or the rightmost tile must be turned sideways, respectively. Just like the rule introduced in Section 1.1 requiring each player’s discards to be arranged neatly in rows of six, these requirements are strictly tied to the <i>furiten</i> rules explained later.

### 1.2.2.2 <i>Pon</i>

Using the same hand as before:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z</m-tehai></p>

If <strong>any other player</strong> discards a tile of which you already hold a pair (for instance, <m-pai>7p</m-pai>), you can call “<strong><i>pon</i></strong>” to form a triplet:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s5z5z5z　7p7p-7p</m-tehai> (a <i>pon</i> from <i>toimen</i>)</p>

At this point, to enter <i>tenpai</i>, you have no choice but to discard <m-pai>3s</m-pai> and accept an bad shape (<i>gukei</i>). However, waiting on <i>kan-</i>2/8 is relatively strong among bad shape, especially during the early stage of the round. After you make your discard, regardless of whose turn it originally was, play immediately skips to the turn of <i>shimoncha</i>. Theoretically, if you keep calling <i>pon</i> on tiles discarded by <i>shimoncha</i>, you can keep <i>kamicha</i> and <i>toimen</i> permanently skipped, never letting them touch the wall.

> In Japanese, entering <i>tenpai</i> after making a <i>chī</i> or <i>pon</i> call is commonly referred to as <i>chīten</i> or <i>ponten</i>, respectively.

An triplet formed by calling a tile is called an <strong>open triplet</strong> (<i>minkō</i>), whereas a triplet completed entirely within your hand through your own draws is called a <strong>concealed triplet</strong> (<i>ankō</i>). Logically, the terms “open sequence” and “concealed sequence” could exist, but because no game mechanics differentiate them, you will rarely hear them used.

The priority of a <i>pon</i> (or <i>kan</i>) call is higher than that of a <i>chī</i>. If <i>shimoncha</i> calls <i>chī</i> on a tile, but <i>toimen</i> or <i>kamicha</i> simultaneously calls <i>pon</i> (or <i>kan</i>), the <i>pon</i> (or <i>kan</i>) takes precedence, and the <i>chī</i> is canceled. Naturally, a declaration of a win by discard (<i>ron</i>) has the highest priority over <i>chī</i>, <i>pon</i>, and <i>kan</i>.

### 1.2.2.3 <i>Kuikae</i>

If you intentionally break up an already completed meld in your hand to make a <i>chī</i> or <i>pon</i> call, Japanese mahjong enforces the following restrictions:

- It is forbidden to immediately discard the exact same tile that you just used to make a call. For example, you cannot call <i>pon</i> on a <m-pai>1s</m-pai> with a <m-pai>1s1s</m-pai> pair and then immediately discard the fourth <m-pai>1s</m-pai> from your hand; likewise, you cannot call <i>chī</i> on a <m-pai>4p</m-pai> with a <m-pai>5p6p</m-pai> <i>tātsu</i> and then immediately discard another <m-pai>4p</m-pai> from your hand.
- If you call <i>chī</i> using a <i>ryanmen tātsu</i>, it is also forbidden to immediately discard the tile on the opposite end of that sequence (referred to as a tile on the same “<i>suji</i>”). For example, if you call <i>chī</i> on a <m-pai>4p</m-pai> with a <m-pai>5p6p</m-pai>, you cannot immediately discard a <m-pai>7p</m-pai>.

These illegal moves are collectively known as the prohibition of <strong><i>kuikae</i></strong>[^kuikae]. When aiming for certain <i>yaku</i>, you must be careful not to commit a foul by violating this rule.

[^kuikae]: Literally “eating and swapping.”

Since Japanese mahjong does not actively encourage making a <i>kan</i> call as much as Chinese mahjong does, and because <i>kan</i> involves the uniquely Japanese <i>dora</i> mechanics, we will cover it later in 1.2.4. Next, we will introduce several of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i>. Before we do, however, we need to understand the overall flow of a game of Japanese mahjong so that we can properly introduce our very first <i>yaku</i>.

## 1.2.3 <i>Yakuhai</i> and <i>Tanyao</i>

The most prevalent format in Japanese mahjong is the <strong><i>hanchansen</i></strong> (“<i>hanchan</i> match”)[^hanchan], also called a <strong><i>tonnansen</i></strong> (“East-South match”):

[^hanchan]: “<i>Han</i>” literally means “half”; “<i>chan</i>” literally means “dealer position” and has been shifted here to mean “a full game.” Playing through the East and South rounds is called a “half-game” because it is based on the traditional full game consisting of East, South, West, and North rounds (<i>īchan</i>), a format that is no longer popular in Japan.

::: theorem
1. Randomly determine a <strong>starting dealer</strong> (<i>chīcha</i>), who serves as the dealer (East) for the first round.
2. Starting from <i>chīcha</i>, the dealer position rotates counterclockwise among the four players (though the physical seats do not change). The matches from the “East 1” round (<i>ton</i> 1-<i>kyoku</i>) to the “East 4” round (<i>ton</i> 4-<i>kyoku</i>) make up <strong>the East Round</strong> (<strong><i>tonba</i></strong>).
3. The dealer position completes another full rotation from “South 1” (<i>nan</i> 1-<i>kyoku</i>) to “South 4” (<i>nan</i> 4-<i>kyoku</i>), making up <strong>the South Round</strong> (<strong><i>nanba</i></strong>).
:::

The terms “East Round” and “South Round” mean that during the East Round, <m-pai>1z</m-pai> is the <strong>round wind</strong> (<i>bakaze</i> or <i>chanfon</i>), and during the South Round, <m-pai>2z</m-pai> becomes the round wind. This introduces our very first <i>yaku</i>:

<YakuName base="場風牌" ruby="bakazehai" lang="en" /> (**Round Wind Tile**): A hand containing a triplet of the round wind tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.

Advancing from East 4 to South 1 is called <i>nannyū</i> (“entering South”), where the round wind changes from East to South. South 4 is known as the **all last** (<i>ōrasu</i>), and its conclusion marks the end of the match (unless the conditions to end the game have not been met, in which case general rules dictate entering the West Round, which will be detailed later).

Second only in popularity to the half-game is the <strong><i>tonpūsen</i></strong> (“East-only match”), where only the East Round is played.

Next, let let us look at our second <i>yaku</i>. Regardless of the round wind, <m-pai>1z</m-pai> for the dealer (East player) of a particular round, <m-pai>2z</m-pai> for the South player, and so on, is called that player’s <strong>self wind</strong> (<i>jikaze</i>) or <strong>seat wind</strong> (<i>menfon</i>).

<YakuName base="自風牌" lang="en" ruby="jikazehai" /> (**Self Wind Tile**): A hand containing a triplet of your self wind tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.

Before announcing their score, a winning player can announce each of their achieved <i>yaku</i> one by one (though this is not permitted under strict competitive rules). For the two types of wind <i>yaku</i> mentioned above, players generally declare the name of the wind tile itself, namely “<i>ton</i>” (East), “<i>nan</i>” (South), “<i>shā</i>” (West), or “<i>pē</i>” (North).

For the dealer during an East Round, or the South player during a South Round, the two <i>yaku</i> above will inevitably be achieved at the same time if a triplet is formed. In this case, they stack to become 2 <i>han</i>, a combination known as <strong><i>renfōnpai</i></strong> (“stacked wind tile”). When declaring this <i>yaku</i>, players say “<i>dabu-ton</i>” (double-East) or “<i>dabu-nan</i>” (double-South).

On a related note, let us introduce the final type of <i>yaku</i> that can be achieved directly with a single triplet, which remains valid regardless of your seat wind or the round wind:

<YakuName base="三元牌" lang="en" ruby="sangenpai" /> (**Dragon Tiles**): A hand containing a triplet of <m-pai>5z</m-pai>, <m-pai>6z</m-pai>, or <m-pai>7z</m-pai>. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Holding two such triplets is worth 2 <i>han</i>[^sangen].

[^sangen]: Completing triplets of all three types results in the “Big Three Dragons,” which will not be discussed for now.

When declaring this <i>yaku</i>, the name of the tile itself is used as the <i>yaku</i> name: “<i>haku</i>” (White), “<i>hatsu</i>” (Green), or “<i>chun</i>” (Red). The hand shown at the beginning of Section 1.2.2 features this “<i>haku</i>.”

Seat winds, round winds, and dragon tiles are collectively referred to as <strong><i>yakuhai</i></strong> (also known as <i>fanpai</i>). While it is ideal to draw the tiles yourself to form a concealed triplet (<i>ankō</i>), even if you only start with a pair, calling <i>pon</i> immediately secures 1 <i>han</i>. It remains valid even if you call <i>chī</i> or <i>pon</i> on other tiles first and wait to call your value tiles later (<strong><i>atozuke</i></strong>), which drastically accelerates the speed of your hand. Because of its incredible utility, it has long been nicknamed the “<i>tokkyūken</i>” (“express ticket”). <i>Yakuhai</i> is one of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i> in Japanese mahjong.

However, speaking of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i>, we must mention:

<YakuName base="断么九" lang="en" ruby="tanyao" />[^tanyao] (**All Simples**): A hand composed entirely of suited tiles from 2 to 8, containing no 1s, 9s, or honor tiles. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.

[^tanyao]: The <i>yaku</i> names in this tutorial follow standard convention, using their common pronunciations as *gikun*. For instance, All Simples has the full formal name *tanyaochū*, but since it is rarely used in actual play, it is omitted here.

“<strong><i>Yaochūhai</i></strong>” refer to 1s, 9s, and honor tiles altogether, including numbered 1s and 9s as well as word tiles. On the other hand, numbered 1s and 9s alone are called “<strong>terminals</strong>” (<i>rōtōhai</i>). When your hand lacks a pair of value tiles, and other terminal or honor tiles are scarce or isolated (floating tiles, or <i>ukihai</i>), <i>tanyao</i> will always be by your side. As a popular saying on the Chinese version of <i>Mahjong Soul</i> loading screen goes: “<i>Gōngfu zài gāo, yě pà duànyāo</i>” (No matter how high your kung fu, you still fear <i>tanyao</i>). This refers to how rushing a 1-<i>han</i> cheap hand of <i>tanyao nomi</i>[^nomi] makes it possible to win the hand before opponents who are struggling to build expensive hands.

[^nomi]: <i>Nomi</i> means “only.” <i>Tanyao nomi</i> means a hand with “only <i>tanyao</i> and no other <i>yaku</i>.”

## 1.2.4 Dead Wall, Kan, and Related Rules

### 1.2.4.1 Dead Wall and Dora

Not every tile in the wall is drawable. Seven two-tile stacks (14 tiles total) are fixed as the **dead wall** (<ruby>王<rt>ワン</rt>牌<rt>パイ</rt></ruby>). Inside it, two stacks are **rinshan tiles** (<ruby>嶺上牌<rt>リンシャンパイ</rt></ruby>) used after kans, and five stacks are **dora indicator stacks**. At hand start, only the top tile of the first indicator stack is revealed. The remaining nine tiles become relevant as kans and rīchi conditions are met, explained step by step later.

<figure class="main-figure">
  <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Wampai%28mahjong%291.jpg" alt="Dead wall with one dora indicator revealed." width="450" class="image" />
  <figcaption class="image-caption">Left two stacks are rinshan tiles. To avoid collapse, the first top rinshan tile is often moved below. Further left is wall break start; further right is haitei position, the last live draw. Image source: <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Wampai(mahjong)1.jpg">バーレンムール</a>, <a href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0">CC BY-SA 4.0</a>, via Wikimedia Commons</figcaption>
</figure>

Each dora tile in your winning hand adds 1 han. At hand start there is one dora type (up to four copies). It is determined from the indicator as follows:

- If indicator is a suited tile such as <m-pai>1p</m-pai>, next rank in same suit (<m-pai>2p</m-pai>) is dora. If indicator is 9, then 1 is dora.
- If indicator is wind tile: <m-pai>1z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>2z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>3z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>4z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>1z</m-pai>.
- If indicator is dragon tile: <m-pai>5z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>6z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>7z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>5z</m-pai>.

In addition, one red 5 in each suit is also dora, called **aka dora**. If the dora indicator points to 5 as dora, that suit red 5 counts as two dora.

Dora is not a <i>yaku</i>. **Dora without <i>yaku</i> is still not a legal win.** After a legal win, dora is announced together with <i>yaku</i>, for example <i>dora yon</i> for four dora.

### 1.2.4.2 Kan

A <strong><i>kan</i></strong> is a <i>furo</i> formed by creating a **quad** (<i>kantsu</i>) of four identical tiles. While *chii* and *pon* function similarly to drawing a tile (by completing a standard set), a Kan is different. This is because it merely converts a 3-tile triplet into a 4-tile set. Furthermore, when determining yaku, it is still treated as a triplet, meaning **it does not affect the formation of various triplet-based yaku, such as Yaku_hai**. 

As a result, after calling Kan, you must first draw a tile from the wall before discarding. This increases your active hand to 14 tiles, and 15 tiles when winning (and more with subsequent Kans). The tile drawn at this moment is none other than the Rinshanpai (dead wall tile) mentioned earlier. Just like Chi and Pon, the turn then passes to the player to your right (shimoncha).

Each time a Kan is made, an additional upper-row Dora indicator tile is flipped over. The new Dora introduced by this indicator is called **Kan Dora**. Since there are only four Rinshanpai and four Kan Dora indicators available, the maximum number of Kans allowed in a single hand is four.

There are three types of Kan:

- **<ruby>Daiminkan<rt>ダイミンカン</rt></ruby> (Open Kan)**: If you have a triplet of three identical tiles in your closed hand and any other player discards the fourth, you can call "**Kan**", reveal your three tiles, and take the discarded tile to form an open Kan. If you are calling Daiminkan from the player opposite you (toimen), you can choose to turn either the second tile sideways <n-w>（<m-pai>3m3m-3m3m</m-pai>）</n-w> or the third tile sideways <n-w>（<m-pai>3m3m3m-3m</m-pai>）</n-w> (M.League fixes this to the former).
- **<ruby>Shouminkan<rt>ショウミンカン</rt></ruby> (Added Kan)**: Commonly referred to as "**<ruby>Kakan<rt>カカン</rt></ruby>**". After drawing a tile (including a Rinshanpai draw), if you have a tile in your closed hand (including the tile just drawn) that matches an open triplet you have already melded, you can call "**Kan**" and add that tile to the open triplet to convert it into a Kan. The added tile is placed sideways directly on top of the tile that was already turned sideways (e.g., <m-pai style="margin-top: 0.3em;">6z6z6z-6z=</m-pai>).
- **<ruby>Ankan<rt>アンカン</rt></ruby> (Closed Kan)**: After drawing a tile (including a Rinshanpai draw), if you possess four identical tiles within your closed hand (including the tile just drawn), you can call "**Kan**" and reveal the four tiles to form a closed Kan. To distinguish it from an open Kan, either the two outer tiles or the two inner tiles are turned face down (M.League fixes this to the latter) (e.g., <m-pai>006m6m00</m-pai>). **A hand remains closed (menzen) if it has no other open melds besides a closed Kan.** It also counts as a closed triplet (ankou) when calculating yaku such as San'ankou or Suu'ankou.

Under general rules, the procedure for a Kan is as follows: For Daiminkan and Kakan, you call Kan, draw a Rinshanpai, and then flip over the new Dora indicator (Kan Dora) immediately after discarding a tile. On the other hand, for Ankan, you flip over the new Dora indicator immediately upon declaring the Kan (known as *soku-mekuri* or *soku-nori*). In contrast, competitive rules unify the process for all Kans—whether open or closed—to the latter (flipping the indicator immediately upon the Kan's completion).

Here is an occasional yaku associated with Kan:

<YakuName base="Rinshan Kaihou" ruby="リンシャンカイホウ" />: Winning by self-draw (tsumo) on a Rinshanpai. Worth **1 han**.

While it is legally possible to win a hand with nothing but an occasional yaku, aiming for this as a basis for your hand progression is entirely unrealistic in a world without magic.

### 1.2.4.3 Haitei and Houtei

The final drawable tile in the live wall is the **haitei tile** (<ruby>海<rt>ハイ</rt>底<rt>テイ</rt>牌<rt>ハイ</rt></ruby>). By rule, each kan pushes the haitei position one tile earlier. The original last tile loses gameplay function and only maintains the 14-tile dead-wall structure. Historically, the dead wall predates the dora system and originally existed to increase uncertainty.

Another chance <i>yaku</i>:

<YakuName lang="en" base="Haitei Raoyue" ruby="ハイテイラオユエ" />: self-draw win on haitei tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

After drawing haitei, kan is forbidden. If the tile just before haitei is used for kan, that kan removes the original haitei from live status. If the resulting rinshan draw wins, it counts as rinshan kaihou, not haitei raoyue. The two cannot stack.

The discard made after drawing haitei, or after making that pre-haitei kan, is the **houtei tile** (<ruby>河<rt>ホウ</rt>底<rt>テイ</rt>牌<rt>ハイ</rt></ruby>). Calls are forbidden on houtei discard. Another chance <i>yaku</i>:

<YakuName lang="en" base="Houtei Raoyui" ruby="ホウテイラオユイ" />: ron win on houtei tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

## 1.2.5 Dealer Repeat and Game End

### 1.2.5.1 Dealer Repeat

After a hand ends, if dealer remains dealer and seat winds do not rotate, that is a **renchan** (<ruby>連<rt>レン</rt>荘<rt>チャン</rt></ruby>). Conditions are:

- **Agari renchan** (<l-ja>アガリ連荘</l-ja>): dealer wins.
- **Tenpai renchan** (<l-ja>テンパイ連荘</l-ja>): hand ends in draw and dealer is tenpai.

Each renchan adds one **honba** (<ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt>場<rt>ば</rt></ruby>) counter. Example in East 3: first time as dealer is East 3, 0 honba; first repeat becomes East 3, 1 honba; and so on. In hand-shuffled play, dealer places one temporary 100-point stick per honba as marker. On automatic tables the center display shows honba directly.

**When any player wins, extra honba payment applies: on ron, discarder pays honba x 300; on tsumo, each non-winner pays honba x 100.**

If renchan conditions are not met, dealer passes to lower seat. This is called parent flow in Chinese communities.

### 1.2.5.2 End of Game

Using hanchan as example, oorasu is South 4. Under common rules:

- Dealer in oorasu may continue renchan.
  - If renchan conditions fail, game ends (or enters West round, see below).
  - If renchan conditions are met but dealer is already first place, game still ends. This is analogous to renchan and called <i>agari yame</i> or <i>tenpai yame</i>[^yame].
- After oorasu, if no player has reached 30,000 points, game enters **West round**. Round wind becomes <m-pai>3z</m-pai>.
  - In Tenhou and Mahjong Soul, play usually stops by West 4 with no North round. Some traditional rulesets allow North round, and even return to East after North if conditions still fail.

[^yame]: Originally this implied dealer could choose whether to stop. In Tenhou and Mahjong Soul it is typically enforced automatically.
