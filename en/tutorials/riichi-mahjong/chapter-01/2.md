# 1.2 <i>Yaku</i>, Calls, and Rounds

Mahjong has always been a game with countless variations in detailed rules, whether in offline play or on online battle platforms. Therefore, it must be acknowledged that the “general rules” referred to in this tutorial are primarily aligned with two platforms: **<i>Tenhō</i>** ([Official Website](http://tenhou.net/)), which launched in 2006 and is universally recognized for having the highest level of play and the most prestigious <i>dan</i> rankings, and **<i>Mahjong Soul</i>** ([ZH](https://www.maj-soul.com/), [JA](https://mahjongsoul.com/), and [EN](https://mahjongsoul.yo-star.com/) Official Websites), which launched in 2018, shares basically identical rules with <i>Tenhō</i>, and stands as the most popular online mahjong platform today. After all, apart from these two, other platforms that were once popular have all been lost in time, like tears in rain. Meanwhile, the “competitive rules” are primarily modeled after the highly popular **M.League** ([Official Website](https://m-league.jp/)). Although it incorporates <i>akahai</i> and can be seen as a modern competitive rule set that has compromised with widespread parlor rules, we are, after all, a tutorial for the modern era. As for other rule variations that remain fairly common, we will list them separately in a dedicated section.

## 1.2.1 Points

In any form of mahjong, if a winning hand fulfills certain specific patterns or special conditions in addition to the basic winning shape, it often receives extra rewards. The colloquial idiom 清一色 ([MSC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Chinese): <i>qīngyīsè</i>), commonly heard in everyday Chinese to mean “uniformly,” originally refers to a mahjong winning hand composed entirely of a single suit. Known as a Flush in English, this hand also yields a substantial payout in Japanese mahjong. Besides Flush, there are over thirty other specific winning shapes or special conditions in Japanese mahjong, collectively known as **<i>yaku</i>**.[^yaku] The value of a <i>yaku</i> is measured in **<i>han</i>**,[^han] and multiple <i>yaku</i> achieved simultaneously can combine, adding their <i>han</i> values together. In Japanese, <i>yaku</i> achieved through specific hand patterns are called <i>teyaku</i> (hand-based <i>yaku</i>), while those achieved by meeting special conditions are called <i>jōkyō</i> (“circumstantial”) <i>yaku</i>. Among the latter, except for <i>menzen tsumo</i> and <i>rīchi</i>, all are literally <i>gūzen</i> (“coincidental”) <i>yaku</i>.

[^yaku]: Literally “character; role.”

[^han]: Written in <i>kanji</i> as 翻 or 飜. Functionally, it refers to the number of score doublings, though in modern Japanese mahjong, this strict exponential doubling is capped by the <i>mangan</i> (limit hand) system.

The number of <i>han</i> determines the value of the hand, which in turn determines the points of the winning player. Each player’s points at the end of the match determine their final rank, and the final rank determines their mood. At the start of a match of Japanese mahjong, each player most commonly begins with **25,000 points**. A match consists of multiple **hands** (<i>kyoku</i>, see 1.2.3), and points are calculated and exchanged at the end of each hand. Specifically:

- **Non-dealer win by self-draw** (<i>tsumo</i>): The dealer (East) pays the winner 2× the base points,[^kihonten] while the other two non-dealers each pay 1× the base points, totaling 4× the base points for the winner.
- **Non-dealer win by discard** (<i>ron</i>): The player who dealt into the winning hand (**<i>hōjū</i>**) solely pays the winner 4× the base points.
- **Dealer win by self-draw** (<i>tsumo</i>): The three non-dealers each pay the dealer 2× the base points (totaling 1.5× the payout of a non-dealer win).
- **Dealer win by discard** (<i>ron</i>): The player who dealt into the hand solely pays the dealer 6× the base points (1.5× the payout of a non-dealer win).
- **Exhaustive draw** (<i>ryūkyoku</i>, the hand ends with no winner after the wall is drawn): Players who are not in <i>tenpai</i> collectively pay a total penalty of 3,000 points (<i>nōten</i> penalty or <i>nōten bappu</i>), which is then split equally among the players who are in <i>tenpai</i> (see 1.2.5.2 for details).

[^kihonten]: The higher the value of the hand, the higher the “base points” (<i>kihonten</i>). The exact algorithm will be detailed in the section on scoring. Actual payments are rounded up to the nearest hundred, meaning a non-dealer’s payment is not always exactly half of the dealer’s payment.

The scoring of Japanese mahjong, in addition to being heavily influenced by the number of <i>han</i>, also retains the classical <i>fu</i> system. To avoid mental calculations during every hand, players generally memorize a points lookup table, which we will discuss in detail later. For now, let us introduce the chips used in Japanese mahjong to represent a player’s current points—**<i>tenbō</i>** (“point sticks”):

As the game progresses, players estimate the potential value of their hand to calculate the conditions required for a comeback, and declare their points immediately upon winning. Modern Japanese mahjong enforces a one-<i>han</i> minimum constraint (**<i>īhan shibari</i>**). If you do not have any <i>yaku</i>, even if you have formed “four melds and one pair,” you will find that there is no “zero <i>han</i>” column in the points lookup table. In this case, declaring a win means you cannot state valid points, which constitutes a foul (**<i>chombo</i>**). Therefore, in addition to focusing on the winning shape, we must simultaneously keep an eye on the <i>yaku</i> we need to achieve.

## 1.2.2 <i>Chī</i> and <i>Pon</i>

To secure at least one <i>yaku</i>, our strategy can be highly simplified into two directions:

- If you have a good prospect of completing any hand-based <i>yaku</i>, you can utilize the <i>naki</i> mechanics introduced in this section to advance your hand.
- If the prospects for any hand-based <i>yaku</i> are unclear, you should refrain from <i>naki</i>. This state is called **closed hand** (<i>menzenchin</i>[^menzen] or simply <i>menzen</i>). As long as you maintain a closed hand until <i>tenpai</i>, you can utilize the powerful circumstantial <i>yaku</i> known as <i>rīchi</i>, which will be covered in Section 1.3.

[^menzen]: Literally “clear in front of the gate.”

Calling tiles—namely <i>chī</i>, <i>pon</i>, and <i>kan</i>—along with the winning hand shapes, form the core mechanics of all variations of mahjong. In Japanese mahjong, this is collectively referred to as **<i>naki</i>**[^naki] or **<i>fūro</i>**.[^furo] It is also colloquially called <i>kui</i>[^kui] or <i>shikake</i>.[^shikake] Since this has been a core mechanic since the ancient origins of the game, we will explain it before introducing the uniquely Japanese <i>rīchi</i> mechanic. Take this hand as an example:

[^naki]: Literally “calling.”

[^furo]: Literally “tile set exposure.”

[^kui]: Literally “eating.”

[^shikake]: Literally “setting up.”

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z</m-tehai><br><small>Spoiler alert: This hand already has one guaranteed <i>yaku</i> from the <i>haku</i> triplet.</small></p>

Here, a two-tile combination that is just one tile away from becoming a sequence, such as <m-pai>7m9m</m-pai> or <m-pai>2s3s</m-pai>, is called a **<i>tātsu</i>**. The former is a <i>kanchan tātsu</i>, while the latter is a <i>ryanmen tātsu</i>.

> A hand that is just one tile away from <i>tenpai</i>, as in this example, is called <i>īshanten</i>. Following the same logic, a hand two tiles away is <i>ryanshanten</i>, and three tiles away is <i>sanshanten</i>.

### 1.2.2.1 <i>Chī</i>

If the **player to your left** (<i>kamicha</i>) discards a tile that can form a sequence (<i>shuntsu</i>) with a <i>tātsu</i> in your hand—such as an <m-pai>8m</m-pai> for this particular hand—you can immediately declare “**<i>chī</i>**.” You then reveal the <m-pai>7m9m</m-pai> from your hand and take the left player’s <m-pai>8m</m-pai> to form a sequence:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z　8m-7m9m</m-tehai></p>

The rest of your tiles remain upright and concealed. Normally, you would draw a tile on your turn, but since calling a tile effectively counts as drawing one, you proceed immediately to choose a tile from your hand and discard it. In this position, discarding either <m-pai>2s</m-pai> or <m-pai>3s</m-pai> will put you in <i>tenpai</i>, so you would generally discard <m-pai>2s</m-pai> to keep a good wait shape. Afterward, your right-hand player (<i>shimocha</i>) takes their turn—though they could also choose to <i>chī</i> your <m-pai>2s</m-pai>.

Once a <i>fūro</i> is formed—including via <i>pon</i> and <i>kan</i>—you can no longer discard tiles from it or swap tiles within it. In Japanese mahjong, <i>fūro</i> like the <m-pai>8m-7m9m</m-pai> shown in the diagram must be exposed together at the bottom-right corner of your call area. Furthermore, the <m-pai>8m</m-pai> must be placed on the left side of the meld and turned sideways to indicate that it came from the player to your left. Similarly, <i>pon</i> and <i>kan</i> can be made from any player: if the tile comes from the player opposite you or to your right, the middle tile or the rightmost tile must be turned sideways, respectively. Just like the rule introduced in Section 1.1 requiring each player’s discards to be arranged neatly in rows of six, these requirements are strictly tied to the <i>furiten</i> rules explained later.

### 1.2.2.2 <i>Pon</i>

Using the same hand as before:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z</m-tehai></p>

If **any other player** (<i>tācha</i>) discards a tile of which you already hold a pair (for instance, <m-pai>7p</m-pai>), you can call “**<i>pon</i>**” to form a triplet:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s5z5z5z　7p7p-7p</m-tehai> (a <i>pon</i> from <i>toimen</i>)</p>

At this point, to enter <i>tenpai</i>, you have no choice but to discard <m-pai>3s</m-pai> and accept a bad shape (<i>gukei</i>). However, waiting on <i>kan-</i>2/8 is relatively strong among bad shapes, especially during the early stage of the hand. After you make your discard, regardless of whose turn it originally was, play immediately skips to the player to your right. Theoretically, if you keep calling <i>pon</i> on tiles discarded by the player to your right, you can keep the player to your left and across from you permanently skipped, never letting them touch the wall.

> Entering <i>tenpai</i> after making a <i>chī</i> or <i>pon</i> call is commonly referred to as <i>chīten</i> or <i>ponten</i>, respectively.

A triplet formed by calling a tile is called an **open triplet** (<i>minkō</i>), whereas a triplet completed entirely within your hand through your own draws is called a **closed triplet** or **concealed triplet** (<i>ankō</i>). Logically, the terms “open sequence” and “closed sequence” could exist, but because no game mechanics differentiate them, you will rarely hear them used.

The priority of a <i>pon</i> (or <i>kan</i>) call is higher than that of a <i>chī</i>. If the player to the right calls <i>chī</i> on your tile, but the player across or to your left simultaneously calls <i>pon</i> (or <i>kan</i>), the <i>pon</i> (or <i>kan</i>) takes precedence, and the <i>chī</i> is canceled. Naturally, a declaration of a win by discard (<i>ron</i>) has the highest priority over <i>chī</i>, <i>pon</i>, and <i>kan</i>.

### 1.2.2.3 <i>Kuikae</i>

If you intentionally break up an already completed meld in your hand to make a <i>chī</i> or <i>pon</i> call, Japanese mahjong enforces the following restrictions:

- It is forbidden to immediately discard the exact same tile that you just used to make a call. For example, you cannot call <i>pon</i> on a <m-pai>1s</m-pai> with a <m-pai>1s1s</m-pai> pair and then immediately discard the fourth <m-pai>1s</m-pai> from your hand; likewise, you cannot call <i>chī</i> on a <m-pai>4p</m-pai> with a <m-pai>5p6p</m-pai> <i>tātsu</i> and then immediately discard another <m-pai>4p</m-pai> from your hand.
- If you call <i>chī</i> using a <i>ryanmen tātsu</i>, it is also forbidden to immediately discard the tile on the opposite end of that sequence (referred to as a tile on the same “<i>suji</i>”). For example, if you call <i>chī</i> on a <m-pai>4p</m-pai> with a <m-pai>5p6p</m-pai>, you cannot immediately discard a <m-pai>7p</m-pai>.

These illegal moves are collectively known as the prohibition of **<i>kuikae</i>**.[^kuikae] When aiming for certain <i>yaku</i>, you must be careful not to commit a foul by violating this rule.

[^kuikae]: Literally “eating and swapping.”

Since Japanese mahjong does not actively encourage making a <i>kan</i> call as much as Chinese mahjong does, and because <i>kan</i> involves the uniquely Japanese <i>dora</i> mechanics, we will cover it later in 1.2.4. Next, we will introduce several of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i>. Before we do, however, we need to understand the overall flow of a game of Japanese mahjong so that we can properly introduce our very first <i>yaku</i>.

## 1.2.3 <i>Yakuhai</i> and <i>Tanyao</i>

The most prevalent format in Japanese mahjong is the **<i>hanchansen</i>** (“<i>hanchan</i> match”),[^hanchan] also called a <i>tonnansen</i> (“East-South match”):

[^hanchan]: “<i>Han</i>” (半) literally means “half”; “<i>chan</i>” literally means “dealer position” and has been shifted here to mean “a full game.” Playing through the East and South rounds is called a “half-game” because it is based on the traditional full game (<i>īchan</i>) consisting of East, South, West, and North rounds, a format that is no longer popular in Japan.

::: theorem
1. Randomly determine the first dealer (<i>chīcha</i>), who starts as East for the opening hand.
2. The dealer position rotates counterclockwise (passing to the player’s right) after each hand, while physical seats remain fixed. The hands from East 1 (<i>ton</i> 1-<i>kyoku</i>) to East 4 (<i>ton</i> 4-<i>kyoku</i>) comprise the **East round** (<i>tonba</i>).
3. The dealer position completes another full rotation from South 1 (<i>nan</i> 1-<i>kyoku</i>) to South 4 (<i>nan</i> 4-<i>kyoku</i>), comprising the **South round** (<i>nanba</i>).
:::

The terms “East round” and “South round” mean that during the East round, <m-pai>1z</m-pai> is the **round wind** (<i>bakaze</i> or <i>chanfon</i>) or **prevalent wind**, and during the South round, <m-pai>2z</m-pai> becomes the round wind. This introduces our very first <i>yaku</i>:

<YakuName base="場風牌" ruby="Bakazehai" lang="en" /> (**Round Wind Tile**): A hand containing a triplet of the round wind tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.

Advancing from East 4 to South 1 is called <i>nannyū</i> (“entering South”), where the round wind changes from East to South. South 4 is known as the **all last** (<i>ōrasu</i>), and its conclusion marks the end of the match (unless the conditions to end the game have not been met, in which case general rules dictate entering the West round, which will be detailed later).

Second only in popularity to the half-game is the **<i>tonpūsen</i>** (“East-only match”), where only the East round is played.

Next, let us look at our second <i>yaku</i>. Regardless of the round wind, <m-pai>1z</m-pai> for the dealer (east player) of a particular hand, <m-pai>2z</m-pai> for the south player, and so on, is called that player’s **self wind** (<i>jikaze</i>) or **seat wind** (<i>menfon</i>).

<YakuName base="自風牌" lang="en" ruby="Jikazehai" /> (**Self Wind Tile**): A hand containing a triplet of your self wind tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.

Before announcing their points, a winning player can announce each of their achieved <i>yaku</i> one by one (though this is not permitted under competitive rules). For the two types of wind <i>yaku</i> mentioned above, players generally declare the name of the wind tile itself, namely “<i>ton</i>” (East), “<i>nan</i>” (South), “<i>shā</i>” (West), or “<i>pē</i>” (North).

For the dealer during an East round, or the south player during a South round, the two <i>yaku</i> above will inevitably be achieved at the same time if a triplet is formed. In this case, they stack to become 2 <i>han</i>, a combination known as **<i>renfōnpai</i>** (“double wind tile”). When declaring this <i>yaku</i>, players say “<i>dabu-ton</i>” (double-East) or “<i>dabu-nan</i>” (double-South).

On a related note, let us introduce the final type of <i>yaku</i> that can be achieved directly with a single triplet, which remains valid regardless of your seat wind or the round wind:

<YakuName base="三元牌" lang="en" ruby="Sangenpai" /> (**Dragon Tiles**): A hand containing a triplet of <m-pai>5z</m-pai>, <m-pai>6z</m-pai>, or <m-pai>7z</m-pai>. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Holding two such triplets is worth 2 <i>han</i>.[^sangen]

[^sangen]: Completing triplets of all three types results in the “Big Three Dragons,” which will not be discussed for now.

When declaring this <i>yaku</i>, the name of the tile itself is used as the <i>yaku</i> name: “<i>haku</i>” (White), “<i>hatsu</i>” (Green), or “<i>chun</i>” (Red). The hand shown at the beginning of Section 1.2.2 features this “<i>haku</i>.”

Seat winds, round winds, and dragon tiles are collectively referred to as **<i>yakuhai</i>** (also known as <i>fanpai</i>). While it is ideal to draw the tiles yourself to form a closed triplet (<i>ankō</i>), even if you only start with a pair, calling <i>pon</i> immediately secures 1 <i>han</i>. It remains valid even if you call <i>chī</i> or <i>pon</i> on other tiles first and wait to call your <i>yakuhai</i> later (**<i>atozuke</i>**), which drastically accelerates the speed of your hand. Because of its incredible utility, it has long been nicknamed the “express ticket” (<i>tokkyūken</i>). <i>Yakuhai</i> is one of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i> in Japanese mahjong.

However, speaking of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i>, we must mention:

<YakuName base="断么九" lang="en" ruby="Tanyao" />[^tanyao] (**All Simples**): A hand composed entirely of suited tiles from 2 to 8, containing no 1s, 9s, or honor tiles. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.

[^tanyao]: <i>Tan</i> means “to eliminate,” and <i>yao</i> is short for <i>yaochū</i>. The <i>yaku</i> names in this tutorial follow standard convention by using their common, everyday terms. For instance, while All Simples has the full formal name <i>tanyaochū</i>, it is rarely used in actual play and is therefore omitted here.

**<i>Yaochūhai</i>** (“<i>yaochū</i> tiles,” or terminals and honors) refer to 1s, 9s, and honor tiles altogether. On the other hand, numbered 1s and 9s alone are called **<i>rōtōhai</i>**, or **terminals** in English. When your hand lacks a pair of <i>yakuhai</i>, and other terminal or honor tiles are scarce or isolated (floating tiles, or <i>ukihai</i>), <i>tanyao</i> will always be by your side. As a popular saying on the Chinese version of <i>Mahjong Soul</i> loading screen goes: “<i>Gōngfu zài gāo, yě pà duànyāo</i>” (No matter how high your kung fu, you still fear <i>tanyao</i>). This refers to how rushing a 1-<i>han</i> cheap hand of <i>tanyao nomi</i>[^nomi] makes it possible to win the hand before opponents who are struggling to build expensive hands.

[^nomi]: <i>Nomi</i> means “only.” <i>Tanyao nomi</i> means a hand with “only <i>tanyao</i> and no other <i>yaku</i>.”

## 1.2.4 The Dead Wall, <i>Kan</i>, and Others

### 1.2.4.1 The Dead Wall and <i>Dora</i>

In practice, not all tiles in the wall are destined to enter a player’s hand. There are exactly 7 stacks of 14 tiles that are strictly set aside and cannot be drawn through normal play; this is called the **dead wall** (<i>wanpai</i>). In contrast, the section of the wall from which players normally draw is called the **live wall** in English. Within the dead wall, there are 2 stacks (4 tiles) known as **<i>rinshanpai</i>**[^rinshanpai]—used for drawing a tile after declaring a <i>kan</i>, as explained below—and 5 stacks (10 tiles) of <strong><i>dora</i> indicators</strong> (<i>dora hyōjihai</i>). Initially, only the top tile of the very first stack among these five is turned face up. As for the remaining 9 tiles, the top row is related to <i>kan</i> calls, and the bottom row is related to <i>rīchi</i>; we will introduce their functions progressively.

[^rinshanpai]: Literally “tiles on top of the mountain peak.”

<figure class="main-figure">
  <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Wampai%28mahjong%291.jpg" alt="Out of the 7 stacks of the dead wall, the top tile of the third stack is revealed." width="450" class="image" />
  <figcaption class="image-caption">The 2 stacks on the left are the <i>rinshanpai</i>. To prevent them from accidentally being knocked over, the topmost <i>rinshanpai</i> at the very edge is moved down beforehand. Further to the left of the diagram is the <i>kaimen</i> position (where initial hands are dealt), and to the right is the <i>haitei</i> position (the very last drawable tile in the live wall). </figure>

For each **<i>dora</i>** tile contained in a winning hand, an additional 1 <i>han</i> is awarded. At the start of every hand, 1 type of tile (4 tiles in total) is designated as the <i>dora</i>, determined as follows:

- If the <i>dora</i> indicator is a suit tile (e.g., <m-pai>1p</m-pai>), the next numerical tile of the same suit (<m-pai>2p</m-pai>) becomes the <i>dora</i>. If the <i>dora</i> indicator is a 9, the 1 of the same suit becomes the <i>dora</i>.
- If the <i>dora</i> indicator is a wind tile: <m-pai>1z</m-pai> → <m-pai>2z</m-pai> → <m-pai>3z</m-pai> → <m-pai>4z</m-pai> → <m-pai>1z</m-pai>.
- If the <i>dora</i> indicator is a dragon tile: <m-pai>5z</m-pai> → <m-pai>6z</m-pai> → <m-pai>7z</m-pai> → <m-pai>5z</m-pai>.

Additionally, the <i>akahai</i> (red 5-<i>man</i>, red 5-<i>sō</i>, and red 5-<i>pin</i>, one tile each) also function as permanent <i>dora</i>, known as <strong>red <i>dora</i></strong> (<i>akadora</i>). If the <i>dora</i> indicator happens to be a 4, the red 5 of that suit counts as two <i>dora</i> simultaneously.

Note that <i>dora</i> does not count as a <i>yaku</i>; **having <i>dora</i> without a valid <i>yaku</i> will not allow you to declare a win**. However, upon winning, you declare the number of <i>dora</i> alongside your <i>yaku</i> using the phrase “<i>dora</i> + number,” such as “<i>dora yon</i>” (four <i>dora</i>). Having one or two <i>dora</i> can also be colloquialized as “<i>dora</i>” or “<i>dora-dora</i>” respectively.

### 1.2.4.2 <i>Kan</i>

Declaring a **<i>kan</i>** is a call used to form a **quad** (<i>kantsu</i>)—four identical tiles. While making a <i>chī</i> or <i>pon</i> call functions exactly like drawing a tile, a <i>kan</i> does not. This is because a <i>kan</i> merely converts a three-tile meld into a four-tile meld (for the purpose of scoring and <i>yaku</i> determination, a quad is still treated as a triplet; that is to say, **it does not affect the completion of <i>yakuhai</i> or any other triplet-based <i>yaku</i>**). Therefore, you must draw an extra tile from the <i>rinshanpai</i> before making a discard. This changes your active hand size to 14 tiles during normal play, and 15 tiles when declaring a win (increasing further with subsequent quad). Just like with <i>chī</i> and <i>pon</i>, play continues with the player to your right after a <i>kan</i> discard.

Every time a <i>kan</i> is declared in a hand, an additional top-row <i>dora</i> indicator is turned face up. The new <i>dora</i> revealed this way is called **<i>kandora</i>**. If there are two identical tiles among the revealed <i>dora</i> indicators, each corresponding <i>dora</i> tile in your hand is counted twice, and so on. Since there are only four <i>rinshanpai</i> and four corresponding top-row <i>dora</i> indicators, a maximum of four <i>kan</i> can be declared in total per hand.

There are three types of <i>kan</i>:

- **Open quad** (<i>daiminkan</i>): If you hold three identical tiles in your hand and any other player discards the fourth, you can call “**<i>kan</i>**.” You then reveal the three tiles from your hand and take the discarded tile to form an open quad. If you make an open quad from the player across, you can turn either the second tile <n-w>(<m-pai>3m3m-3m3m</m-pai>)</n-w> or the third tile <n-w>(<m-pai>3m3m3m-3m</m-pai>)</n-w> sideways (M.League rules fix this to the former method).
- **Added open quad** (<i>shōminkan</i> or more commonly <i>kakan</i>): After drawing a tile (including a <i>rinshanpai</i>), if you have a tile in your hand (or have just drawn it) that matches an open triplet already in your call area, you can call “**<i>kan</i>**.” You add this tile to the open triplet to convert it into an open quad. The added tile is placed sideways directly on top of the tile that was already turned sideways in the triplet, like so: <m-pai style="margin-top: 0.3em;">6z6z6z-6z=</m-pai>.
- **Closed quad** (<i>ankan</i>): After drawing a tile (including a <i>rinshanpai</i>), if you hold four identical tiles in your hand (or have just drawn the fourth), you can call “**<i>kan</i>**.” You reveal the four tiles to form a closed quad. To distinguish it from an open quad, either the outer two tiles or the middle two tiles are placed face down (M.League rules fix this to the latter method), like so: <m-pai>006m6m00</m-pai>. **If you have no other <i>fūro</i>, making closed quads still keeps your hand closed (<i>menzen</i>).** It also counts as a concealed triplet when scoring <i>yaku</i> such as Three Concealed Triplets or Four Concealed Triplets.

Under general rules, the procedure for <i>kan</i> is as follows:

- **Open quad (including added open quad)**: After declaring, you draw a <i>rinshanpai</i> and discard. The new <i>dora</i> is revealed immediately after that discard.
- **Closed quad**: The new <i>dora</i> is revealed immediately upon declaring (known as <i>sokumekuri</i> or <i>sokunori</i>).

Under competitive rules, however, the procedure is unified to follow the latter method for both types of quads.

There is a coincidental <i>yaku</i> associated with <i>kan</i>:

<YakuName base="嶺上開花" lang="en" ruby="Rinshan kaihō"/> (**After a <i>Kan</i> / Dead Wall Draw**): Winning by self-draw (<i>tsumo</i>) using a <i>rinshanpai</i>. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Abbreviated as <i>rinshan</i>.

While it is possible to win a hand relying solely on coincidental <i>yaku</i>, in a world without magic, building a hand around the expectation of them is completely unrealistic.

### 1.2.4.3 <i>Haitei</i> and <i>Hōtei</i>

The very last drawable tile in the live wall is called the **<i>haiteihai</i>**.[^haiteihai] According to the rules, each time a <i>kan</i> is declared, the position of the <i>haiteihai</i> shifts one tile forward. The original <i>haiteihai</i> then loses its function and is absorbed into the dead wall simply to maintain the strict 14-tile count. In fact, the history of the dead wall predates that of <i>dora</i>; it was originally introduced solely to increase the unpredictability of the game.

There is a coincidental <i>yaku</i> associated with this tile:

<YakuName base="海底撈月" lang="en" ruby="Haitei raoyue"/> (**Under the Sea**): Winning by self-draw (<i>tsumo</i>) on the <i>haiteihai</i>. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Abbreviated as <i>haitei</i>.

It is strictly forbidden to declare a <i>kan</i> after drawing the <i>haiteihai</i>. If you declare a <i>kan</i> on the tile immediately preceding the <i>haiteihai</i>, that action effectively consumes the original <i>haiteihai</i> by forcing it into the dead wall. If you happen to win by self-draw on the resulting <i>rinshanpai</i>, it does not count as <i>haitei raoyue</i>. In other words, <i>rinshan</i> and <i>haitei</i> cannot stack (combine) with each other.

The final discarded tile—either after drawing the <i>haiteihai</i> or after declaring a <i>kan</i> on the tile right before it—is called the **<i>hōteihai</i>**.[^hoteihai] Other players are forbidden from making any <i>chī</i>, <i>pon</i>, or <i>kan</i> on the <i>hōteihai</i>. This gives rise to another coincidental <i>yaku</i>:

[^hoteihai]: Literally “tile at the bottom of the river.”

<YakuName base="河底撈魚" lang="en" ruby="Hōtei raoyui"/> (**Under the River**): Winning by discard (<i>ron</i>) on the <i>hōteihai</i>. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Abbreviated as <i>hōtei</i>.

## 1.2.5 <i>Renchan</i>, Exhaustive Draws, and Ending the Game

### 1.2.5.1 <i>Renchan</i>

When a hand ends and the current dealer retains their position for the next round—meaning the seat winds of all four players remain unchanged—it is called **<i>renchan</i>** (“dealer repeat”). The conditions for a <i>renchan</i> are as follows:

- **<i>Agari renchan</i>** (“<i>renchan</i> upon winning”): The dealer wins the hand and continues.
- **<i>Tenpai renchan</i>** (“<i>renchan</i> upon <i>tenpai</i>”): If the hand ends in an exhaustive draw and the dealer is in <i>tenpai</i> (see 1.2.5.2), they also continue.

When a <i>renchan</i> occurs, the number of **<i>honba</i>** increases by one. For example, during East 3, the dealer’s very first hand is called East 3, <i>Honba</i> 0. Their first **<i>renchan</i>** advances the game to East 3, <i>Honba</i> 1, and so forth. In tiles-only games played by hand, the dealer places a single 100 stick on the table as a visual marker for each added <i>honba</i>. These are called <i>tsumibō</i> or <i>babō</i>. Automatic tables display the <i>honba</i> count directly in the center of the table.

**When any player wins a hand, if it is a win by discard (<i>ron</i>), the player who dealt in must pay an additional 300 points per <i>honba</i>. If it is a win by self-draw (<i>tsumo</i>), the other three players must each pay an additional 100 points per <i>honba</i>.**

If the conditions for a <i>renchan</i> are not met at the end of a hand, the dealer position passes to the player to their right. In Japanese, this is referred to as <i>oya-nagare</i>, and non-dealers may describe this action as <i>oya o nagasu</i>.

### 1.2.5.2 Exhaustive Draws

In the case of an exhaustive draw, players in <i>tenpai</i> must reveal their hands and declare “**<i>tenpai</i>**,” while players not in <i>tenpai</i> must place their tiles face down and declare “**<i>nōten</i>**.” In reality, if you have not declared <i>rīchi</i>, you can intentionally declare <i>nōten</i> even if you are technically in <i>tenpai</i>, but online platforms like <i>Tenhō</i> and <i>Mahjong Soul</i> automatically force a <i>tenpai</i> declaration if your hand qualifies. As previously mentioned, a total of 3,000 points in <i>nōten</i> penalties (<i>nōten bappu</i>) is collected evenly from the <i>nōten</i> players and distributed equally among the <i>tenpai</i> players. Naturally, this results in the following exchanges:

- If zero players are in <i>tenpai</i>, no points are exchanged.
- If one player is in <i>tenpai</i>, the three <i>nōten</i> players pay 1,000 points each to that <i>tenpai</i> player.
- If two players are in <i>tenpai</i>, each of the two <i>nōten</i> players pays 1,500 points to one of the <i>tenpai</i> players.
- If three players are in <i>tenpai</i>, the lone <i>nōten</i> player pays 1,000 points to each of the three <i>tenpai</i> players.
- If all four players are in <i>tenpai</i>, no points are exchanged.

Regarding what qualifies as <i>tenpai</i> at an exhaustive draw, the rule states that a <i>tenpai</i> with no <i>yaku</i> (<i>yaku</i>-less <i>tenpai</i>, <i>keishiki tenpai</i> or simply <i>keiten</i>) as well as a hand whose winning tiles are completely depleted across the discards and hands (<i>karaten</i> or empty <i>tenpai</i>) **both count as valid <i>tenpai</i>**. The only exception that does not count as <i>tenpai</i> is when your hand is inherently impossible to win on judging strictly from the tiles held in your hand (under general rules, this only considers your concealed tiles, whereas competitive rules take <i>fūro</i> into account as well):

A <i>renchan</i> is not the only event that increases the <i>honba</i>; an exhaustive draw does so as well. **If a round ends in an exhaustive draw, the dealer position rotates, but the <i>honba</i> increases by one.** For example, an exhaustive draw at East 1, <i>Honba</i> 0 leads to East 2, <i>Honba</i> 1; an exhaustive draw at East 4, <i>Honba</i> 3 leads to South 1, <i>Honba</i> 4, and so forth.

### 1.2.5.3 Ending the Game

Finally, let us look at when a game comes to an end. Taking a <i>hanchan</i> as our example, the all last will be South 4. Under general rules, the match ends when any of the following conditions are met:

- If any player’s points drop below zero (<i>hakoten</i>) after the points are settled at the end of any hand, the game ends immediately. This is known as being bankrupt (**<i>tobi</i>**).
- The dealer in the all last (<i>rasuoya</i>) can also achieve a <i>renchan</i>. The game continues until they fail to meet the <i>renchan</i> conditions, at which point the game ends (or enters a West round, see below).
  - However, even if they meet the <i>renchan</i> conditions, if the dealer is already in top (first) place at that moment, the game still ends automatically. Mirroring <i>renchan</i>, these are called <i>agari yame</i> (“stopping upon winning”) and <i>tenpai yame</i> (“stopping upon <i>tenpai</i>”).[^yame]
- If no player has reached 30,000 points at the end of the all last, the game **enters the West round** (<i>shānyū</i>), where the round wind becomes <m-pai>3z</m-pai>. Once in the West round, the match ends instantly as a sudden-death format the moment anyone exceeds 30,000 points.
  - While <i>Tenhō</i> and <i>Mahjong Soul</i> max out at West 4 and do not allow entering a North round, traditional rules may feature a North round, or even progress to a “returning East round” (<i>kaeriton</i>) if conditions remain unmet at the end of North 4.

Under competitive rules, there is no bankruptcy (play continues even with negative points), no West round, and neither <i>agari yame</i> nor <i>tenpai yame</i> exists (as long as the dealer meets the conditions to continue, they are forced to do so).

[^yame]: These terms originally meant that the dealer could “voluntarily choose” whether to end the game, but in platforms like <i>Tenhō</i> and <i>Mahjong Soul</i>, they function as described here.
