# 1.2 <i>Yaku</i>, Calls, and Round Flow

Mahjong has countless rule variations, both offline and on online platforms. In this tutorial, general rules primarily follow two platforms: <strong><i>Tenhō</i></strong> ([official site](http://tenhou.net/)), launched in 2006 and widely regarded as the most competitive ranked environment, and **Mahjong Soul** ([CN](https://www.maj-soul.com/) / [JP](https://mahjongsoul.com/) / [EN](https://mahjongsoul.yo-star.com/)), launched in 2018 with rules broadly aligned with Tenhou. Competitive rules in this tutorial mainly follow **M.League** ([official site](https://m-league.jp/)). Even though modern pro rules already include compromises such as red fives, this is a modern tutorial, so that is the baseline. Other common rule differences will be listed in a separate section.

## 1.2.1 Scores

In any mahjong ruleset, a hand that not only forms a legal winning shape but also satisfies specific patterns or conditions receives extra value. In Japanese mahjong, these scoring elements are called <strong><i>yaku</i></strong>[^yaku]. Their value is measured in <strong><i>han</i></strong>[^han], and multiple <i>yaku</i> can stack by addition. <i>Yaku</i> achieved by hand structure are often called **shape yaku** (<i>teyaku</i>), while <i>yaku</i> achieved by special circumstances are **situational yaku**. Among situational <i>yaku</i>, all but <i>menzen tsumo</i> and <i>riichi</i> are, literally, chance-only <i>yaku</i>.

[^yaku]: Literally “character.”

[^han]: Literally “doubles.”

Han determines hand value, and therefore determines points won. Final points at the end of a game decide placement (<ruby>順位<rt>じゅんい</rt></ruby>). Under common rules, each player starts a game with **25,000 points** (the starting score). A game consists of multiple rounds (see 1.2.3), and points are transferred after every round:

- **Non-dealer tsumo win**: dealer pays 2x base points[^kihonten], each non-dealer pays 1x; winner receives 4x total.
- **Non-dealer ron win**: the discarder (<ruby>放銃<rt>ほうじゅう</rt></ruby>[^hoju]) alone pays 4x.
- **Dealer tsumo win**: each of the three non-dealers pays 2x (total is 1.5x a non-dealer win).
- **Dealer ron win**: discarder alone pays 6x (also 1.5x a non-dealer win).
- **<ruby>流局<rt>りゅうきょく</rt></ruby>** (draw, no one wins before live wall ends): non-tenpai players pay a total 3,000-point no-ten penalty (<ruby>不聴<rt>ノーテン</rt>罰符<rt>バップ</rt></ruby>)[^bappu], split among tenpai players.

[^kihonten]: Larger hand value gives larger base points. We will cover exact calculation later. Payments are rounded up to the nearest hundred, so non-dealer payment is not always exactly half of dealer payment.

[^hoju]: Also called <l-ja>振り込む</l-ja>. In Chinese communities this is often called dealing in.

[^bappu]: Despite the historical term 罰符, this is a point penalty and unrelated to the fu count discussed below.

Japanese point calculation depends not only on han, but also on the classical **fu** (<ruby>符<rt>フ</rt></ruby>) count. To avoid recalculating every hand, players memorize point tables. We will cover this in detail later. For now, here are the score sticks used to represent current points (持ち点): **tenbo** (<ruby>点<rt>てん</rt>棒<rt>ぼう</rt></ruby>).

During play, players estimate possible hand values for comeback lines and for immediate point declaration after a win. Modern Japanese mahjong applies a one-<i>yaku</i> minimum (<strong><l-ja><ruby>一<rt>イー</rt>翻<rt>ハン</rt></ruby>縛り</l-ja></strong>): if your hand has no <i>yaku</i>, even a complete four-melds-one-pair shape cannot legally win. There is no zero-han row in the table, and declaring a win in that state is a rules violation (chombo[^chonbo]). So while building shape, you must always track how your hand will satisfy <i>yaku</i>.

[^chonbo]: Historically written 錯和 or 冲和. Originally a false-win infraction, now a general term for major table violations.

## 1.2.2 Chii and Pon

To secure at least one <i>yaku</i>, an intentionally simplified plan usually branches into two approaches:

- If a specific shape <i>yaku</i> path is promising, use the open-call system explained in this section.
- If no shape <i>yaku</i> path is clear, keep the hand closed. This is **<ruby>門<rt>メン</rt>前<rt>ゼン</rt>清<rt>チン</rt></ruby>** (short form <i>menzen</i>[^menzen]). A closed tenpai hand can then use the very powerful situational <i>yaku</i> <i>riichi</i> in 1.3.

[^menzen]: Chinese shorthand often says 門清, but in Japanese 門清 can specifically imply closed full flush. This tutorial uses menzen to avoid ambiguity.

Like winning shape itself, chii / pon / kan are core mechanics across mahjong families. In Japanese mahjong they are collectively called calls (<i>naku</i>) or **<ruby>副<rt>フー</rt>露<rt>ロ</rt></ruby>**, also called 食う or 仕掛ける. We explain this core mechanism before the Japan-specific riichi system. Consider this hand:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z</m-tehai><br><small>Spoiler: this hand already has one <i>yaku</i>, White dragon.</small></p>

Here, <m-pai>7m9m</m-pai> and <m-pai>2s3s</m-pai> are two-tile proto-melds one tile away from a sequence. These are called **<ruby>搭子<rt>ターツ</rt></ruby>**[^tatu]. The former is a closed wait proto-shape, the latter is a two-sided proto-shape.

> A hand state one tile away from tenpai is called <i>iishanten</i>. By extension there are also <i>ryanshanten</i>, <i>sanshanten</i>, and so on.

[^tatu]: If written in Japanese kanji, 塔子 is also common.

### 1.2.2.1 Chii

If the player on your left discards a tile that completes one of your sequence proto-shapes, for example <m-pai>8m</m-pai> for this hand, you may immediately call **チー** (<i>chii</i>), reveal <m-pai>7m9m</m-pai>, and take that <m-pai>8m</m-pai> to form a sequence:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z　8m-7m9m</m-tehai></p>

All other tiles remain upright and concealed (the narrow sense of hand tiles). Normally you would draw after the left player discards, but chii effectively replaces that draw. So after chii you discard directly. In this example, discarding <m-pai>2s</m-pai> or <m-pai>3s</m-pai> both reach tenpai, and <m-pai>2s</m-pai> is usually preferred for better shape. Then play continues with the next player, who may in turn chii your discard if legal.

As with pon and kan, once tiles are moved to the open-meld area, they cannot be taken back into concealed hand management. In Japanese mahjong, open melds such as <m-pai>8m-7m9m</m-pai> are placed in a fixed called area at the right side of your rack. The called tile is laid sideways to show source direction: left position for chii from left player; middle or right positions for calls from opposite or right player. Like six-wide discard rows, this visual encoding matters for furiten-related rules later.

### 1.2.2.2 Pon

Same starting hand:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s7p7p5z5z5z</m-tehai></p>

If **any opponent** discards a tile you already have as a pair, for example <m-pai>7p</m-pai>, you may call **ポン** (<i>pon</i>) to form a triplet:

<p><m-tehai>1m2m3m7m9m2s2s3s5z5z5z　7p7p-7p</m-tehai> (example shown as pon from opposite seat)</p>

From here, reaching tenpai forces a discard of <m-pai>3s</m-pai> into a weak-shape wait. Still, a closed wait on 2 or 8 can be a relatively acceptable weak shape, especially in early turns. After your discard, play always passes to your lower seat. In theory, repeated pons on that player can deny draws to players further around the table.

> Tenpai reached after chii or pon is often called <i>chiiten</i> or <i>ponten</i>.

A called triplet is a <ruby>明<rt>ミン</rt>刻<rt>コー</rt></ruby>, while a self-made concealed triplet is an <ruby>暗<rt>アン</rt>刻<rt>コー</rt></ruby>. In principle one could also say open sequence or closed sequence, but those terms are rarely needed.

Pon (and kan) has higher priority than chii. If the lower seat declares chii but another player declares pon or kan on the same discard, pon/kan takes precedence and chii is canceled. Ron has higher priority than all of them.

### 1.2.2.3 Kuikae

If you intentionally break a completed meld in hand to call and then discard in a way that restores the previous structure, Japanese rules prohibit the following:

- After chii/pon, discarding the same tile as the called tile. Example: call pon on <m-pai>1s</m-pai> with <m-pai>1s1s</m-pai>, then discard the fourth <m-pai>1s</m-pai>; or call chii with <m-pai>5p6p</m-pai> on <m-pai>4p</m-pai>, then discard another <m-pai>4p</m-pai>.
- After chii with a two-sided proto-shape, discarding the tile at the opposite end of the same <ruby>筋<rt>スジ</rt></ruby>. Example: chii <m-pai>4p</m-pai> with <m-pai>5p6p</m-pai>, then discard <m-pai>7p</m-pai>.

This prohibition is called **kuikae**. Be careful when pursuing specific shape <i>yaku</i> to avoid accidental infractions.

Japanese mahjong does not encourage kan as much as many Chinese local rulesets do, and kan is tightly tied to the dora system. So we explain it later. Before introducing the most frequent <i>yaku</i>, we first need the round structure, which we cover next.

## 1.2.3 Yakuhai and Tanyao

Japanese mahjong most commonly uses the **hanchan format** (<ruby>半荘戦<rt>ハンチャンセン</rt></ruby>):

::: theorem
1. Randomly choose one player as the **starting dealer** (<l-ja><ruby>起<rt>チー</rt>家<rt>チャ</rt></ruby></l-ja>), dealer of East 1.
2. Starting from that player, dealer passes counter-clockwise through all four players, producing East 1 to East 4, called the **East round** (<ruby>東<rt>トン</rt>場<rt>バ</rt></ruby>).
3. Then another full dealer cycle is played as South 1 to South 4, called the **South round** (<ruby>南<rt>ナン</rt>場<rt>バ</rt></ruby>).
:::

East round and South round refer to the current **round wind** (<ruby>場<rt>ば</rt>風<rt>かぜ</rt></ruby>, also 圈風). In East round, <m-pai>1z</m-pai> is round wind. In South round, <m-pai>2z</m-pai> is round wind. That gives us the first <i>yaku</i>:

<YakuName lang="en" base="Round Wind" ruby="バカゼハイ" />: triplet of current round wind. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

Transition from East 4 to South 1 is called <ruby>南入<rt>ナンニュウ</rt></ruby>. South 4 is called **oorasu**[^orasu], and game end is checked there (unless end conditions fail and play continues into West round under common rules, detailed later).

[^orasu]: <i>oorasu</i>, from Japanese English all last.

The second most common format after hanchan is **tonpuusen** (<ruby>東<rt>トン</rt>風<rt>プウ</rt>戦<rt>セン</rt></ruby>), which plays East round only.

Now the second <i>yaku</i>. In any given hand, <m-pai>1z</m-pai> for East seat, <m-pai>2z</m-pai> for South seat, and so on, are each player local **seat wind** (<ruby>自<rt>じ</rt>風<rt>かぜ</rt></ruby>, also 門風).

<YakuName lang="en" base="Seat Wind" ruby="ジカゼハイ" />: triplet of your seat wind. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

Before declaring points, a winner may announce <i>yaku</i> one by one in common rules (not allowed in M.League rules). For the two wind <i>yaku</i> above, players simply call the wind itself: <ruby>東<rt>トン</rt></ruby>, <ruby>南<rt>ナン</rt></ruby>, <ruby>西<rt>シャー</rt></ruby>, or <ruby>北<rt>ペー</rt></ruby>.

For East dealer in East round, South seat in South round, and similar cases, seat wind and round wind can coincide. Then both <i>yaku</i> apply for 2 han (<ruby>二<rt>リャン</rt>翻<rt>ハン</rt></ruby>) and this is called **double wind** (<ruby>連風牌<rt>レンフォンパイ</rt></ruby>). Table callouts are often <i>dabu-ton</i> or <i>dabu-nan</i>[^double].

[^double]: <i>dabu</i> is a clipped form of double.

The last one-han triplet-based <i>yaku</i> is always available regardless of winds:

<YakuName lang="en" base="Dragon Tiles" ruby="サンゲンパイ" />: triplet of <m-pai>5z</m-pai> or <m-pai>6z</m-pai> or <m-pai>7z</m-pai>. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>. Two dragon triplets give 2 han[^sangen].

[^sangen]: Three dragon triplets is the yakuman Daisangen, covered later.

Callout naming is again the tile itself: <ruby>白<rt>ハク</rt></ruby>, <ruby>發<rt>ハツ</rt></ruby>, <ruby>中<rt>チュン</rt></ruby>. The example hand at the start of 1.2.2 already had White.

Seat wind, round wind, and dragon tile <i>yaku</i> are collectively **<ruby>役<rt>やく</rt>牌<rt>はい</rt></ruby>** (<i>yakuhai</i>), also called 翻牌. Concealed triplet is best, but even just a pair can be quickly converted by pon for guaranteed 1 han. You can also call other tiles first and later call yakuhai pair to complete <i>yaku</i> (atozuke). This greatly accelerates hand speed, so yakuhai is one of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i>.

Still, when discussing highest-frequency <i>yaku</i>, one more cannot be skipped:

<YakuName lang="en" base="Tanyao" ruby="タンヤオ" />[^tanyao]: hand contains only suited tiles 2 to 8, with no terminals and no honors. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

[^tanyao]: As usual in this tutorial, <i>yaku</i> names follow standard Japanese reading conventions. The full form tanyaochuu exists but is rarely used.

In Japanese mahjong, **yaochuuhai** (<ruby>么九牌<rt>ヤオチューパイ</rt></ruby>) means terminals and honors together, not terminals alone. If one specifically means 1 and 9 suited tiles, that is **routouhai** (<ruby>老<rt>ロウ</rt>頭<rt>トウ</rt>牌<rt>ハイ</rt></ruby>). When your hand lacks yakuhai pairs and your remaining terminals/honors are sparse or isolated, tanyao often becomes the practical line. Mahjong Soul loading tips joke that no matter how strong your skill is, tanyao is still scary: a fast <i>tanyao nomi</i>[^nomi] one-han hand can outrun players forcing expensive hands.

[^nomi]: <i>nomi</i> means only. <i>Tanyao nomi</i> means a win with tanyao as the sole <i>yaku</i>.

## 1.2.4 Dead Wall, Kan, and Related Rules

### 1.2.4.1 Dead Wall and Dora

Not every tile in the wall is drawable. Seven two-tile stacks (14 tiles total) are fixed as the **dead wall** (<ruby>王<rt>ワン</rt>牌<rt>パイ</rt></ruby>). Inside it, two stacks are **rinshan tiles** (<ruby>嶺上牌<rt>リンシャンパイ</rt></ruby>) used after kans, and five stacks are **dora indicator stacks**. At hand start, only the top tile of the first indicator stack is revealed. The remaining nine tiles become relevant as kans and riichi conditions are met, explained step by step later.

  <figcaption class="image-caption">Left two stacks are rinshan tiles. To avoid collapse, the first top rinshan tile is often moved below. Further left is wall break start; further right is haitei position, the last live draw. Image source: <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Wampai(mahjong)1.jpg">バーレンムール</a>, <a href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0">CC BY-SA 4.0</a>, via Wikimedia Commons</figcaption>
</figure>

Each dora tile in your winning hand adds 1 han. At hand start there is one dora type (up to four copies). It is determined from the indicator as follows:

- If indicator is a suited tile such as <m-pai>1p</m-pai>, next rank in same suit (<m-pai>2p</m-pai>) is dora. If indicator is 9, then 1 is dora.
- If indicator is wind tile: <m-pai>1z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>2z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>3z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>4z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>1z</m-pai>.
- If indicator is dragon tile: <m-pai>5z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>6z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>7z</m-pai> -> <m-pai>5z</m-pai>.

In addition, one red 5 in each suit is also dora, called **aka dora**. If the dora indicator points to 5 as dora, that suit red 5 counts as two dora.

Dora is not a <i>yaku</i>. **Dora without <i>yaku</i> is still not a legal win.** After a legal win, dora is announced together with <i>yaku</i>, for example <i>dora yon</i> for four dora.

### 1.2.4.2 Kan

Kan is an open or concealed declaration of a four-of-a-kind, called **<ruby>槓<rt>カン</rt>子<rt>ツ</rt></ruby>**. Chii and pon effectively replace one draw, but kan does not, because it upgrades a three-tile meld into a four-tile meld and still counts as a triplet-type unit for <i>yaku</i> purposes. So after kan, you first take a supplement draw, then discard. During that process your hand may temporarily be 14 tiles, and a completed winning hand may reach 15 tiles if a kan is present. Supplement draws come from rinshan. As with chii and pon, after your discard turn passes to lower seat.

Each kan adds one new dora indicator on the upper row. That newly revealed dora is called **kan dora**. Since there are only four extra rinshan draws and four extra indicator slots, total kans in one hand are capped at four.

Kan has three forms:

- **<ruby>大明槓<rt>ダイミンカン</rt></ruby>** (open kan from discard): if any opponent discards a tile you already hold as concealed triplet, call **カン** (<i>kan</i>) and expose triplet plus that discard. If called from opposite seat, both <m-pai>3m3m-3m3m</m-pai> and <m-pai>3m3m3m-3m</m-pai> display conventions are used (M.League fixes the former).
- **<ruby>小明槓<rt>ショウミンカン</rt></ruby>** (added kan, usually <ruby>加<rt>カ</rt>槓<rt>カン</rt></ruby>): after a draw, including rinshan draw, if concealed hand contains a tile that matches an exposed triplet, call **カン** and add it on top of the sideways tile in that triplet, e.g. <m-pai style="margin-top: 0.3em;">6z6z6z-6z=</m-pai>.
- **<ruby>暗槓<rt>アンカン</rt></ruby>** (concealed kan): after a draw, including rinshan draw, if concealed hand holds four identical tiles, call **カン** and expose the quad. To distinguish from open kan, either both side tiles or middle two tiles are flipped face-down (M.League uses middle two), e.g. <m-pai>006m6m00</m-pai>. **A hand with only concealed kan and no other calls is still treated as closed.** It also counts as concealed triplet material for <i>yaku</i> such as sanankou and suuankou.

Under common rules, daiminkan and kakan flow as draw from rinshan, discard, then reveal new dora indicator. Ankan often reveals new dora immediately after declaration (mekuri, immediate activation). Competitive rules often unify all kan types to immediate reveal.

There is one chance <i>yaku</i> related to kan:

<YakuName lang="en" base="嶺上開花" ruby="Rinshan kaihō" />: self-draw win on rinshan tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

A chance <i>yaku</i> alone can still satisfy legality, but in practical play it is never a reliable primary plan.

### 1.2.4.3 Haitei and Houtei

The final drawable tile in the live wall is the **haitei tile** (<ruby>海<rt>ハイ</rt>底<rt>テイ</rt>牌<rt>ハイ</rt></ruby>). By rule, each kan pushes the haitei position one tile earlier. The original last tile loses gameplay function and only maintains the 14-tile dead-wall structure. Historically, the dead wall predates the dora system and originally existed to increase uncertainty.

  <figcaption class="image-caption">Image source: manga Saki volume 5, hand 37</figcaption>
</figure>

Another chance <i>yaku</i>:

<YakuName lang="en" base="Haitei Raoyue" ruby="ハイテイラオユエ" />: self-draw win on haitei tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

After drawing haitei, kan is forbidden. If the tile just before haitei is used for kan, that kan removes the original haitei from live status. If the resulting rinshan draw wins, it counts as rinshan kaihou, not haitei raoyue. The two cannot stack.

The discard made after drawing haitei, or after making that pre-haitei kan, is the **houtei tile** (<ruby>河<rt>ホウ</rt>底<rt>テイ</rt>牌<rt>ハイ</rt></ruby>). Calls are forbidden on houtei discard. Another chance <i>yaku</i>:

<YakuName lang="en" base="Houtei Raoyui" ruby="ホウテイラオユイ" />: ron win on houtei tile. Worth <strong class="colored">1 han</strong>.

## 1.2.5 Dealer Repeat and Game End

### 1.2.5.1 Dealer Repeat

After a hand ends, if dealer remains dealer and seat winds do not rotate, that is a **renchan** (<ruby>連<rt>レン</rt>荘<rt>チャン</rt></ruby>). Conditions are:

- **Agari renchan** (<l-ja>アガリ連荘</l-ja>): dealer wins.
- **Tenpai renchan** (<l-ja>テンパイ連荘</l-ja>): hand ends in draw and dealer is tenpai.

Each renchan adds one **honba** (<ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt>場<rt>ば</rt></ruby>) counter. Example in East 3: first time as dealer is East 3, 0 honba; first repeat becomes East 3, 1 honba; and so on. In hand-shuffled play, dealer places one temporary 100-point stick per honba as marker. On automatic tables the center display shows honba directly.

**When any player wins, extra honba payment applies: on ron, discarder pays honba x 300; on tsumo, each non-winner pays honba x 100.**

If renchan conditions are not met, dealer passes to lower seat. This is called parent flow in Chinese communities.

### 1.2.5.2 End of Game

Using hanchan as example, oorasu is South 4. Under common rules:

- Dealer in oorasu may continue renchan.
  - If renchan conditions fail, game ends (or enters West round, see below).
  - If renchan conditions are met but dealer is already first place, game still ends. This is analogous to renchan and called <i>agari yame</i> or <i>tenpai yame</i>[^yame].
- After oorasu, if no player has reached 30,000 points, game enters **West round**. Round wind becomes <m-pai>3z</m-pai>.
  - In Tenhou and Mahjong Soul, play usually stops by West 4 with no North round. Some traditional rulesets allow North round, and even return to East after North if conditions still fail.

[^yame]: Originally this implied dealer could choose whether to stop. In Tenhou and Mahjong Soul it is typically enforced automatically.
