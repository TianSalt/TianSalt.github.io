# 1.3 <i>Rīchi</i>

We have finally arrived at the section that delivers on the title. In this <i>rīchi</i> mahjong, <i>rīchi</i> is not merely a distinctive mechanic—it is the core mechanism compared to other forms of mahjong. As previously stated, <i>rīchi</i> is a circumstantial <i>yaku</i> that can be used simply by being in <i>tenpai</i> with a closed hand. This not only opens a path for hands without any hand-based <i>yaku</i> to quietly advance while staying closed, but also stacks with hands that already possess hand-based <i>yaku</i>. Moreover, Japanese mahjong has many **closed-hand <i>yaku</i>** (<i>menzenyaku</i>) that only count for closed-hand wins, causing these <i>yaku</i> to appear more often together with <i>rīchi</i>. This section will introduce the <i>rīchi</i> mechanism and more <i>yaku</i>, but first, TianSalt would like to revisit the topic of points. After all, Japanese mahjong is a game of competing for final rank, and rank is determined by held points. To gain a concrete understanding of how much a hand can score—tactically often called <i>daten</i>—we will first explain scoring above <i>mangan</i>. This part of the calculation is the simplest, and the one that newcomers tend to learn first. Note that this section focuses only on general rules; differences from competitive rules will be covered in the next section.

## 1.3.1 <i>Mangan</i>

In practice, we will ignore the base points (<i>kihonten</i>) explained in 1.2.1 and look only at the points lookup table. However, in principle, when base points reach 2,000, this is called <i>mangan</i> (limit hand). In classical mahjong, <i>mangan</i> was the absolute ceiling for scoring. Later, with score inflation, the modern mechanism became: when base points exceed 2,000, <i>fu</i> is no longer considered and only <i>han</i> matters—up to 5 <i>han</i>, base points are capped at 2,000, while hands of 6–7 <i>han</i> have base points fixed at 3,000, 8–10 <i>han</i> at 4,000, 11–12 <i>han</i> at 6,000, and so on. As we will learn in the next section, below <i>mangan</i>, each <i>han</i> does literally double the score,[^han] but above <i>mangan</i>, the increase slows dramatically. Specifically, the points lookup table for above <i>mangan</i> is as follows:

## 1.3.2 <i>Rīchi</i>

<i>Rīchi</i> (also written “reach,” though this is not its etymological origin) is the most distinctive mechanic in Japanese mahjong. When in <i>tenpai</i> with a closed hand (<i>menzen</i>), you can declare <i>rīchi</i>. Specifically, after any draw, if the following conditions are simultaneously met:

1. Closed hand (no <i>chī</i>, <i>pon</i>, or open <i>kan</i>).
2. Already in <i>tenpai</i>.
3. Held points are at least 1,000<small> (if the rules allow bankruptcy)</small>.
4. At least 4 tiles remain drawable from the wall<small> (competitive rules do not impose this limit, i.e., “<i>rīchi</i> with no remaining turns” is permitted; however, you cannot declare <i>rīchi</i> after drawing the <i>haiteihai</i>)</small>.

You may:

::: theorem
1. Announce “**<i>rīchi</i>**.”
2. Discard the tile that puts you in <i>tenpai</i>, placing it sideways in your discard pile; this is called the <i>rīchi</i> declaration tile (<i>rīchi sengenpai</i>) <small>(if this tile is called by another player, your next discard is also placed sideways; if another player wins on it, skip step 3)</small>.
3. Pay one 1,000 stick as **<i>kyōtaku</i>** (“deposit”), placing it in the center of the table; this is called the <i>rīchi</i> stick (<i>rīchibō</i>).
4. Maintain a closed hand; until the end of this hand, you cannot choose which tile to discard and must discard whatever you draw (<i>tsumogiri</i>[^tumogiri]).
:::

[^tumogiri]: Conversely, discarding a tile from your hand other than the one just drawn is called <i>tedashi</i>. To distinguish the two, Japanese mahjong generally requires you to complete your discard first, then organize the newly drawn tile into your hand. Using the <i>kotegaeshi</i> technique to quietly mix them up is considered poor etiquette.

**Every player can declare <i>rīchi</i>; all <i>kyōtaku</i> belongs to the winner of that hand. If the hand ends in an exhaustive draw, <i>kyōtaku</i> continues to accumulate until a winner appears, who then takes all <i>kyōtaku</i> from this and previous hands. If exhaustive draws continue until the end of the match, <i>kyōtaku</i> goes to the top player.**

Winning after declaring <i>rīchi</i> constitutes a <i>yaku</i> by itself, and is the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i> in Japanese mahjong:

<p><YakuName base="立直" ruby="Rīchi" lang="en" />: Winning after declaring <i>rīchi</i> in that hand. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. When combined with <i>tanyao</i> etc., it is often abbreviated as <i>men</i>, e.g., <i>rīchi</i> + <i>tanyao</i> is abbreviated as <i>mentan</i>.</p>

Beyond the 1 <i>han</i>, <i>rīchi</i> has two additional potential benefits. The first is the coincidental <i>yaku</i> with the highest frequency:[^f_yaku]

[^f_yaku]: Source: <i>Tenhō</i> <i>yaku</i> statistics (<i>hanchan</i> basis): <span class="url">https://tenhou.net/sc/prof.html</span> (same below).

<p><YakuName base="一発" ruby="Ippatsu" lang="en" /> (One-Shot): Winning within one turn after declaring <i>rīchi</i>. Adds <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.</p>

That is, winning by discard (<i>ron</i>) before your next draw after declaring <i>rīchi</i>, or winning by self-draw (<i>tsumo</i>) on your next draw. It must be noted that within one turn here requires that no <i>chī</i>, <i>pon</i>, or <i>kan</i> (including closed <i>kan</i>) occurs during that interval. The same applies to double <i>rīchi</i> and <i>chīhō</i> discussed later.[^keshi]

[^keshi]: After another player declares <i>rīchi</i>, intentionally calling tiles regardless of risk to negate <i>ippatsu</i> is called <i>ippatsu keshi</i> (“to eliminate <i>ippatsu</i>”).

The second benefit is that only <i>rīchi</i> declarants have the right to flip **<i>uradora</i>** (“hidden <i>dora</i>”) indicator tiles. The <i>dora</i> indicator tiles explained in the previous section are all in the upper row; to distinguish them, they are collectively called **<i>omotedora</i>** (“surface <i>dora</i>”) indicator tiles. <i>Uradora</i> indicator tiles are the tiles directly below the corresponding <i>omotedora</i> indicator tiles; they are revealed only when a <i>rīchi</i> declarant wins, and are effective only for <i>rīchi</i> declarants. <i>Uradora</i> indicator tiles also increase as <i>kan</i> declarations increase on the table, but only the lower tiles of already-revealed upper indicator tiles are effective; newly added <i>uradora</i> from <i>kan</i> are also called <i>kan’uradora</i>. When counting <i>han</i>, <i>omotedora</i> can be called <i>dora</i>, while red <i>dora</i> and <i>uradora</i> are respectively called <i>aka</i> and <i>ura</i>.

Being in <i>tenpai</i> with a closed hand and hand-based <i>yaku</i>, but choosing not to declare <i>rīchi</i>, is called <i>damaten</i> (“silent <i>tenpai</i>”) or simply <i>dama</i>. However, given <i>rīchi</i>’s powerful effectiveness—especially for good-shape <i>tenpai</i> below <i>mangan</i>—declare <i>rīchi</i> immediately upon <i>tenpai</i> (<i>tenpai sokurī</i>) with a closed hand is often the correct play.

There is also another coincidental <i>yaku</i> related to <i>rīchi</i>:

<p><YakuName base="両立直" ruby="Daburu rīchi" lang="en" /> (Double <i>Rīchi</i>): Declaring <i>rīchi</i> immediately upon your first draw, provided no other player has called any tiles since the start of the hand. Worth <strong class="colored">2 <i>han</i></strong>. Does not count <i>rīchi</i>. Abbreviated as <strong><i>daburī</i></strong>.</p>

> Fun fact: Historically, the original meaning of <i>rīchi</i> was declaring <i>tenpai</i> on the first turn, somewhat similar to the modern double <i>rīchi</i>.

## 1.3.3 <i>Furiten</i>

<i>Furiten</i> is a <i>tenpai</i> state where <strong class="colored">winning by discard is prohibited</strong>. There are three triggering conditions:

1. <i>Furiten</i> by discard: The primary meaning. If any of the tiles you are waiting for are among your own discards (including tiles called by other players), you enter <i>furiten</i> status until the condition is no longer satisfied. **Therefore, every time you reach <i>tenpai</i>, it is best to first check whether you are in <i>furiten</i> and can win by discard, to avoid a foul (<i>chonbo</i>).**
2. <i>Furiten</i> after <i>rīchi</i>: If you could win after declaring <i>rīchi</i> but pass (<i>minogasu</i>), you enter <i>furiten</i> status until the end of that hand.
3. Temporary <i>furiten</i>: If you could win by discard but do not, you enter <i>furiten</i> status until your next discard.

Common situations that cause <i>furiten</i>:

- You previously discarded <m-pai>1m</m-pai> from <m-pai>1m4m5m</m-pai>, then successively drew <m-pai>3m</m-pai> <m-pai>6m</m-pai> <m-pai>2m</m-pai>, ultimately becoming a three-sided wait on <m-pai>1m4m7m</m-pai>—<i>furiten</i> due to having discarded <m-pai>1m</m-pai>. Multi-sided waits easily trigger <i>furiten</i>; pay special attention. However, if the wait is very wide, one may directly choose <i>furiten</i> <i>rīchi</i>, aiming only for a self-draw win.
- One side of a multi-sided wait achieves more <i>yaku</i> or even <i>yakuman</i>, so you intentionally pass on the other side’s winning tile.
- You open your hand aiming for <i>tanyao nomi</i>, but end up waiting on <m-pai>1p4p</m-pai> with <m-pai>2p3p</m-pai>. Constrained by the one-<i>han</i> minimum (<i>īhan shibari</i>), whether you draw <m-pai>1p</m-pai> or another player discards it, you have no valid <i>yaku</i> and cannot win, resulting in <i>furiten</i>. **<i>Furiten</i> is judged solely by shape, regardless of <i>yaku</i>.** A hand that can only win on one side is called <i>kataagari</i> (“side winning”).

Because dealing in (<i>hōjū</i>) carries a high penalty, Japanese mahjong heavily emphasizes defense. When another player has declared <i>rīchi</i> or is clearly in <i>tenpai</i>, if you are not yet in <i>tenpai</i> or are two or more tiles away, or if your hand’s value is far lower than theirs, it is common to adopt a stance of completely abandoning the win (<i>betaori</i>, or “folding” in English), even breaking up your hand to prevent dealing in. And <i>furiten</i> is precisely the mechanism most often leveraged for defense. The most basic defensive strategy is to play a <i>rīchi</i> declarant’s **<i>genbutsu</i>**—tiles they have already discarded. <i>Genbutsu</i> are absolute safe tiles (<i>anpai</i>); due to the discard <i>furiten</i> rule, playing another player’s <i>genbutsu</i> can never be won on by discard by that player—we say such a tile “passes” (<i>tōru</i>). The latter two types of <i>furiten</i> can be applied similarly: we can deduce that copying the tile discarded by the player to your left in the same turn will never deal into anyone, and that after one player declares <i>rīchi</i>, all tiles discarded and passed by other players have already passed the <i>rīchi</i> declarant.

## 1.3.4 More <i>Yaku</i>

In this subsection, we will introduce all remaining non-<i>yakuman</i> <i>yaku</i>. The next section will provide a complete <i>yaku</i> reference table. Beyond <i>yakuhai</i> and <i>tanyao</i>, try to notice scenarios where other hand-based <i>yaku</i> have higher chances of being achieved.

### 1.3.4.1 One-<i>han</i> <i>Yaku</i>

<p><YakuName base="搶槓" ruby="Chankan" lang="en" /> (Robbing a Quad / Robbing a <i>Kan</i>): When another player makes an added open <i>kan</i>, and you are waiting on exactly that tile, you may call <i>ron</i>. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.</p>

The rarest coincidental <i>yaku</i>. <i>Chankan</i> is treated as a special win by discard: the declaration is also <i>ron</i>, points are settled as a win by discard, and intentionally passing also results in <i>furiten</i>. If <i>chankan</i> occurs, that <i>kan</i> is treated as not established: no new <i>dora</i> indicator is revealed, and it does not affect <i>ippatsu</i>.

All coincidental <i>yaku</i> (excluding <i>yakuman</i>) have now been covered: <i>rinshan</i>, <i>haitei</i>, <i>hōtei</i>, <i>ippatsu</i>, <i>daburī</i>, and <i>chankan</i>.

<p><YakuName base="門前清自摸和" ruby="Menzenchin tsumohō" lang="en" /> (Fully Concealed Hand): Winning by self-draw with a closed hand. Worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Also known as <i>menzen tsumo</i> or simply <strong><i>tsumo</i></strong>.</p>

One of the highest-frequency <i>yaku</i>. Aside from <i>chankan</i> and <i>hōtei</i>, which are limited to wins by discard, this <i>yaku</i> can combine with all non-<i>yakuman</i> <i>yaku</i>—be careful not to miss it. There is also <i>tsumo nomi</i>: having no hand-based <i>yaku</i> but staying in <i>damaten</i>, then winning by self-draw. In a closed hand, not only does a self-draw win reward 1 <i>han</i>, but a win by discard also rewards 10 <i>fu</i> (see the next section).

<YakuName base="平和" ruby="Pinfu" lang="en" />: The hand simultaneously satisfies

1. No triplets or quads; all four melds are suited sequences.
2. The eyes are not <i>yakuhai</i>; that is, they are suited tiles or “off winds”—wind tiles that are neither round winds nor self winds, also known as “guest winds” (<i>otakaze</i>).
3. The wait pattern is <i>ryanmen</i>.

<strong class="colored">Closed-hand <i>yaku</i></strong>; worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Combined with <i>tanyao</i>, called <i>pintan</i>; with <i>rīchi</i>, called <i>menpin</i>; with both <i>rīchi</i> and <i>tanyao</i>, called <i>mentanpin</i>. <i>Mentanpin</i> + <i>tsumo</i> + <i>dora</i> is a typical <i>mangan</i> hand.

<i>Pinfu</i> (“common hand”) was a core concept in classical mahjong, referring to a winning hand with no added <i>fu</i>. We will mention this further in the next section’s <i>fu</i> calculation. Later, <i>pinfu</i> became a <i>yaku</i>, and when the <i>īhan shibari</i> became widespread, <i>pinfu</i> was changed to closed-hand only to prevent it from being too easy to achieve.

<p><YakuName base="一盃口" ruby="Īpēkō" lang="en" /> (Pure Double Sequence): Containing two identical sequences. <strong class="colored">Closed-hand <i>yaku</i></strong>; worth <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>.</p>

Beginners easily forget that <i>īpēkō</i> is closed-hand only. Since <i>īpēkō</i> is a sequence-based <i>yaku</i>, “1 <i>han</i>, closed-hand only” is essentially the same nature as reduced by 1 <i>han</i> when not closed (see below). In fact, <i>rīchi</i>, <i>tsumo</i>, Seven Pairs, etc., are also closed-hand <i>yaku</i>, but there is no need to particularly note this, so this tutorial does not mention it when introducing them. The only <i>yaku</i> whose closed-hand restriction needs special attention are <i>pinfu</i>, <i>īpēkō</i>, <i>ryanpēkō</i>, and Nine Gates (<i>chūrenpōtō</i>)—just four.

### 1.3.4.2 Seven Pairs

<p><YakuName base="七対子" ruby="Chītoitsu" lang="en" /> (Seven Pairs): Instead of four melds and one pair, having seven distinct pairs also constitutes a winning hand. Worth <strong class="colored">2 <i>han</i></strong>. Abbreviated as <strong><i>chītoi</i></strong>.</p>

<m-tehai>3m3m4m4m7p7p9p9p3s3s1z6z6z　1z</m-tehai>

Among hand-based <i>yaku</i>, it can combine with <i>tanyao</i>, Full Flush, Half Flush, and All Terminals & Honors. To distinguish them, the four-melds-one-pair shape is called a <i>mentsute</i> (“meld-based hand”), and the seven pairs shape is called a <i>toitsute</i> (“pair-based hand”).

### 1.3.4.3 Sequence-Based <i>Yaku</i>

<p><YakuName base="三色同順" ruby="Sanshoku dōjun" lang="en" /> (Mixed Triple Sequence): Each of the three suits contains a sequence of the same numbers. Closed hand <strong class="colored">2 <i>han</i></strong>, open hand <strong class="colored">1 <i>han</i></strong>. Abbreviated as <strong><i>sanshoku</i></strong>.</p>

### 1.3.4.4 Flush-Based <i>Yaku</i>

<p><YakuName base="混一色" ruby="Hon’itsu" lang="en" /> (Half Flush): Suited tiles are all of one type, plus honor tiles. Closed hand <strong class="colored">3 <i>han</i></strong>, open hand <strong class="colored">2 <i>han</i></strong>.</p>

<m-tehai>1m1m4m0m6m6m7m8m4z4z6z6z　4z</m-tehai>

<p><YakuName base="清一色" ruby="Chin’itsu" lang="en" /> (Full Flush): Composed entirely of suited tiles all of one type. Closed hand <strong class="colored">6 <i>han</i></strong>, open hand <strong class="colored">5 <i>han</i></strong>.</p>

<m-tehai>1m1m4m0m6m6m6m7m8m8m8m8m　6m</m-tehai>

Flush-based <i>yaku</i> are collectively called <i>somete</i> (“dyeing hand”). In Japanese, a closed-hand Half Flush is called <i>menhon</i>, and a closed-hand Full Flush is called <i>menchin</i>. Here, <i>men</i> does not refer to <i>rīchi</i>. In fact, since hands of 6 <i>han</i> and 7 <i>han</i> have the same scoring value, making a closed-hand Full Flush often involves choosing <i>damaten</i>.

Full Flush is not only elegant, but frequently produces dazzling multi-sided waits, so much so that dedicated strategy guides exist for it.

### 1.3.4.5 Triplet-Based <i>Yaku</i>

## 1.3.5 Closed <i>kan</i> After <i>Rīchi</i>

Closed <i>kan</i> after <i>rīchi</i> is permitted, but sometimes restricted; examining the details reveals a rather complex set of rules.

<m-tehai>1m1m1m5m6m2p2p3p3p4p4p6s6s　1m</m-tehai> <span class="tehaitext"><i>Kan!</i></span>

Simply converting a closed triplet to a closed <i>kan</i> with nothing else changing, as above, is permitted. As for situations that are not permitted, let us examine some examples.

<m-tehai>4m5m5m5m2p2p3p3p4p4p6s7s8s　5m</m-tehai>

Originally a three-sided wait combining the <m-pai>3m6m</m-pai> <i>ryanmen</i> and <m-pai>4m</m-pai> <i>tanki</i>; making a closed <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>5m</m-pai> would leave only the <m-pai>4m</m-pai> <i>tanki</i> wait. A closed <i>kan</i> that changes the type of wait like this is prohibited.

<m-tehai>5m5m5m5m6m7m3p3p6p7p8p6s7s　8m</m-tehai>

Making a closed <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>5m</m-pai> after drawing <m-pai>8m</m-pai> is called **<i>okurikan</i>** and is prohibited. After <i>rīchi</i>, you may only <i>kan</i> a closed triplet of the tile you just drew!

**The above two situations are what is prohibited in <i>Tenhō</i> and <i>Mahjong Soul</i>**. However, under competitive rules, any closed <i>kan</i> that involves a change in meld composition is entirely prohibited. Interested readers, please expand below to read more.

::: details These examples are prohibited under competitive rules, though you will rarely encounter them in practice—read for fun if you like.

<m-tehai>1p1p1p3p4p4p4p3s4s5s7z7z7z　1p</m-tehai>

Originally waiting on <m-pai>2p3p5p</m-pai>. Making a closed <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>4p</m-pai> changes the wait to <m-pai>2p3p</m-pai>, which is obviously prohibited. But what about making a closed <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>1p</m-pai>? The wait remains <m-pai>2p3p5p</m-pai>, but competitive rules judge based on whether the meld composition has changed, so this is still ruled a foul. We can understand it this way: originally, the three-sided wait was composed of <m-pai>3p</m-pai> <i>tanki</i>, <m-pai>3p4p</m-pai> <i>ryanmen</i>, and <m-pai>1p3p</m-pai> <i>kanchan</i>; after making a <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>1p</m-pai>, the <i>kanchan</i> interpretation is eliminated, leaving only the two-sided combination.

<m-tehai>2m2m2m4m5m5m5m2p3p4p2s3s4s　2m</m-tehai>

Similar to the above. Originally a three-sided wait of <m-pai>3m6m</m-pai> <i>ryanmen</i> + <m-pai>4m</m-pai> <i>tanki</i> + <i>kan-</i><m-pai>3m</m-pai> <i>kanchan</i>; making a closed <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>2m</m-pai> does not change the wait types, but <m-pai>3m</m-pai> can no longer be interpreted as a <i>kanchan</i> wait. Note that the <i>kan</i> also eliminates <i>sanshoku dōjun</i>, revealing the change in meld composition.

<m-tehai>1m1m1m2m2m3m3m3m8p8p8p6z6z</m-tehai>

At first glance, this appears to be a <m-pai>2m6z</m-pai> <i>shanpon</i> wait. But with a keen eye, the character portion can also be viewed as <m-pai>1m2m3m</m-pai> <m-pai>1m2m3m</m-pai> <m-pai>1m3m</m-pai>, waiting on <i>kan-</i><m-pai>2m</m-pai>. Making a <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>1m</m-pai> or <m-pai>3m</m-pai> would leave only the <i>shanpon</i> interpretation, eliminating the possibility of viewing it as a <i>kanchan</i> wait, and is therefore prohibited. However, making a <i>kan</i> of <m-pai>8p</m-pai> is fine.

<m-tehai>2m2m2m4m6m7m8m6p7p8p　003m3m00　2m</m-tehai>

If the possibility of <i>kan-</i><m-pai>3m</m-pai> has already been eliminated by a previous closed <i>kan</i>, can you still <i>kan</i> after drawing <m-pai>2m</m-pai> post-<i>rīchi</i>? This example is debatable.

<m-tehai>2p2p2p3p3p3p4p4p4p7m7m6s7s</m-tehai>

This hand can be viewed either as <i>tanyao</i> + Three Concealed Triplets or as <i>tanyao</i> + <i>pinfu</i> + <i>īpēkō</i>. However, making a closed <i>kan</i> completely eliminates the possibility of the latter interpretation.

<m-tehai>1m1m1m2m4m5m5m6m7m8m9m9m9m</m-tehai>

This final example is permitted under competitive rules, but may be prohibited under traditional rules. Explaining this example requires a spoiler: this is the <i>yakuman</i> Nine Gates (<i>chūrenpōtō</i>). Nine Gates not only requires a closed hand, but also prohibits closed <i>kan</i>. Making a closed <i>kan</i> here eliminates Nine Gates, turning it into an ordinary <i>chin’itsu</i>. Meanwhile, looking closely, to maintain <i>tenpai</i>, you can only interpret this as <i>kan-</i><m-pai>3m</m-pai> wait, so the closed <i>kan</i> does not change the meld composition.
:::
