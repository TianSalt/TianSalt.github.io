# 1.1 Basic Mechanics

<i>Rīchi</i> mahjong is a game with a significant luck component; yet, over hundreds of games, skill differences become readily apparent. This tutorial follows the general Japanese mahjong rules that became more settled in the early 21st century, particularly after the rise of online mahjong. Competitive rules, as represented by M.League (Ｍリーグ), differ from general rules in numerous details; these differences will be noted as appropriate.

Japanese mahjong terminology, like the game itself, was transmitted from China. The pronunciations of many Sino-Japanese terms often resemble older Beijing Mandarin forms. Moreover, many terms have multiple synonymous names. I will endeavor to mention as many as practicable, but omissions are inevitable; please do not be surprised if you encounter variants in other materials.

## 1.1.1 Mahjong Tiles

Standard Japanese mahjong uses 136 tiles (<i>pai</i> or <i>hai</i>): 34 tile types, four copies each. The “flower tiles” (Chinese: <i>huāpái</i>; Japanese: <i>fapai</i> or <i>hanahai</i>), common in parts of southern China, are usually included in commercial Japanese sets but are not used in general rules.

## 1.1.2 How a Hand Proceeds

Let us start after the initial deal (<i>haipai</i>) is complete. At that point, each player has 13 tiles in hand, e.g.

<m-tehai>1m1m6m9m3p4p4p0p9p2s4s1z3z</m-tehai>

After that, players take turns in a draw-discard cycle:

- <strong>Draw</strong> (<i>tsumo</i>): first, draw the next tile from the “wall” (<i>yama</i>[^yama]).
- <strong>Discard</strong> (<i>dahai</i>): then choose one tile from your hand and discard it.

[^yama]: Literally “hill; mountain.”

<m-tehai>1m1m6m9m3p4p4p0p9p2s4s1z3z　</m-tehai><span class="tehaitext">Draw</span> <m-tehai>7z</m-tehai>

↓ Discard <m-pai>9m</m-pai>

<m-tehai>1m1m6m3p4p4p0p9p2s4s1z3z7z</m-tehai>

Your discard pile is called the <i>hō</i> or <i>kawa</i> (literally “river”) in Japanese.

Mahjong is a four-player game. Four players sit around a square table and take turns **counter-clockwise**.

> In Japanese, the other three players at the table are called <i>tācha</i> (literally “the other players”). The player to your left is your <i>kamicha</i> (literally “previous player”); after they play, it is your turn. After your turn, it passes to your right-side <i>shimocha</i> (literally “next player”), and the player across from you is called <i>toimen</i> (literally “opposite”).

In each hand, one player is the **dealer** (<i>oya</i>). The other players are non-dealers (<i>ko</i>). In absolute seat terms, the dealer is the **east player** (<i>toncha</i>); moving counter-clockwise around the table, to their right is the **south player** (<i>nancha</i>), then the **west player** (<i>shācha</i>), and the **north player** (<i>pēcha</i>)—regardless of the actual compass directions.

If any player wins (<i>hōra</i> or <i>agari</i>), that hand (<i>kyoku</i>) ends immediately. Scores are settled, then all tiles are reshuffled for the next hand. Rules about hands will be discussed later; for now, let us first see how to win.

## 1.1.3 Winning and <i>Tenpai</i>

### 1.1.3.1 Winning Hand

Three identical tiles form a **triplet** (<i>kōtsu</i>):

<m-tehai>3p3p3p</m-tehai>, <m-tehai>6z6z6z</m-tehai>&nbsp;...

Three consecutive number tiles in the same suit form a **sequence** (<i>shuntsu</i>):

<m-tehai>3p4p5p</m-tehai>, <m-tehai>6s7s8s</m-tehai>&nbsp;...

Two identical tiles form a **pair** (<i>toitsu</i>):

<m-tehai>9m9m</m-tehai>, <m-tehai>1z1z</m-tehai>&nbsp;...

Triplets and sequences are collectively called **melds** (<i>mentsu</i>). The basic goal in mahjong is a 14-tile winning shape of <strong>4 melds + 1 pair</strong>, such as:

<m-tehai>4m5m6m4p4p4p6p7p8p2s3s4s8s8s</m-tehai>

This is often called **four melds and the eyes**. The “eyes” (<i>jantō</i>, commonly <i>atama</i> “head”) is simply the pair inside a completed winning hand.

### 1.1.3.2 <i>Tenpai</i>

As described above, each player starts with a 13-tile hand. On your turn, you draw a 14th tile and then make a discard to return your hand to 13 tiles. Eventually, a player will complete a 14-tile winning hand upon drawing a tile and declare a win. When your 13-tile hand is just one tile away from winning, this state is called <strong><i>tenpai</i></strong>[^tenpai]:

[^tenpai]: Literally “listen to tiles.”

<m-tehai>3m4m4p4p4p6p7p8p2s3s4s8s8s</m-tehai>

This hand wins on either <m-pai>3m</m-pai> or <n-w><m-pai>6m</m-pai> (wait on “3-6-<i>man</i>”).</n-w> Drawing <m-pai>3m</m-pai> makes <n-w><m-pai>3m4m5m</m-pai>,</n-w> while drawing <m-pai>6m</m-pai> makes <n-w><m-pai>4m5m6m</m-pai>.</n-w> This is a <strong><i>ryanmen</i></strong> (two-sided) wait—where <i>ryan</i> translates to “two” and <i>men</i> to “sides.” There can also be 3-sided or even wider waits, for example:

<m-tehai>3m4m5m6m7m6p7p8p2s3s4s8s8s　</m-tehai><span class="tehaitext">Wait on</span><m-tehai>2m5m8m</m-tehai>

Wait classification matters later for <i>fu</i> calculation. This example is still categorized as <i>ryanmen</i>: if the winning tile is <n-w><m-pai>2m</m-pai>,</n-w> parse it as <m-pai>3m4m　5m6m7m</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>2m5m</m-pai>; if it is <n-w><m-pai>8m</m-pai>,</n-w> parse it as <m-pai>3m4m5m　6m7m</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>5m8m</m-pai>; if it is <n-w><m-pai>5m</m-pai>,</n-w> either interpretation works.

### 1.1.3.3 Declaring a Win

Once you are in <i>tenpai</i>, you can win not only by drawing your winning tile yourself (<i>tsumohō</i> or <i>tsumoagari</i>) but also by winning on another player’s discard (<i>ronhō</i> or <i>ron’agari</i>). In the case above, if you draw <m-pai>4m</m-pai> or <m-pai>7m</m-pai> yourself, you call “<strong><i>tsumo</i></strong>”, reveal your full hand, and settle points to end the hand. If another player discards either of these tiles, you call “<strong><i>ron</i></strong>” to claim the win, followed by the same showdown and scoring procedure.

In Japanese mahjong, completing a winning shape is not enough by itself. You also need at least one valid <i>yaku</i> and must not violate <i>furiten</i> rules. Still, the core is the hand shape, and this tutorial will guide you through those additional requirements naturally.

## 1.1.4 Wait Patterns

Within the standard four-melds-one-pair structure, besides <i>ryanmen</i> waits, there are four other wait (<i>machi</i>) patterns:

- If your hand has three complete melds and one pair, plus two same-suit tiles with one gap between them, that is a <strong><i>kanchan</i></strong>[^kanchan] wait:

  <m-tehai>1s2s3s1p3p1m2m3m3m3m9m9m9m　</m-tehai><span class="tehaitext">Wait on “<i>kan</i>-<m-pai>2p</m-pai>”</span>

  Can you spot which tiles are serving as the eyes here?
  A <i>ryanmen</i> wait has two winning tile types, while a <i>kanchan</i> wait has only one, so it is generally harder to win with.

- If the final incomplete shape is 1-2 or 8-9, that is a <strong><i>penchan</i></strong>[^penchan] wait:

  <m-tehai>8s9s2p3p3p3p4p1z1z1z2z2z2z　</m-tehai><span class="tehaitext">Wait on “<i>pen</i>-<m-pai>7s</m-pai>”</span>

  The tiles are sorted by rank. In dot tiles, this is sequence <m-pai>2p3p4p</m-pai> plus eyes <n-w><m-pai>3p3p</m-pai>.</n-w>

- If the hand already has three melds and the remainder is two pairs, waiting on either tile to make a triplet is a <strong><i>shanpon</i></strong>[^shanpon] wait:

  <m-tehai>5p0p3s4s0s6m7m7m8m8m9m1z1z　</m-tehai><span class="tehaitext">Wait on “<m-pai>5p</m-pai>-<m-pai>1z</m-pai> <i>shanpon</i>”</span>
  The character tiles are actually two sequences.

- If four melds are complete and one lone tile remains, waiting for its match to form the pair is a <strong><i>tanki</i></strong>[^tanki] wait:

  <m-tehai>1s3s3s3s4s4s4s6s6s6s8s8s8s　</m-tehai><span class="tehaitext">Wait on “<m-pai>1s</m-pai> <i>tanki</i>”</span>
  Actually, this example is not purely a <i>tanki</i> wait. This hand also waits on <n-w><m-pai>2s</m-pai>,</n-w> because you can alternatively treat <m-pai>3s3s</m-pai> as the eyes, resulting in a <i>kanchan</i> wait on <n-w><m-pai>2s</m-pai>.</n-w>

[^kanchan]: Literally “inlay tile.”

[^penchan]: Literally “side tiles.”

[^shanpon]: Literally “dual touch.”

[^tanki]: Literally “(to fight) single-handedly on horseback.”

::: details If we replace <m-pai>1s</m-pai> with <m-pai>2s</m-pai> in the last example:<p><m-tehai>2s3s3s3s4s4s4s6s6s6s8s8s8s</m-tehai></p>this becomes a very wide wait. Can you find all winning tiles?<p>(Click to expand answer)</p>

<m-pai>1s2s3s4s</m-pai> — a four-sided wait.

First, treat <m-pai>4s4s4s</m-pai> as a triplet. Then <m-pai>2s3s3s3s</m-pai> can be parsed either as eyes <m-pai>3s3s</m-pai> + <n-w><m-pai>2s3s</m-pai>,</n-w> a <i>ryanmen</i> wait on <n-w><m-pai>1s4s</m-pai>,</n-w> or as triplet <m-pai>3s3s3s</m-pai> + <m-pai>2s</m-pai> <i>tanki</i>.

However, the <m-pai>2s　3s　4s</m-pai> portion can also be parsed as <n-w><m-pai>2s3s4s</m-pai><m-pai>3s3s</m-pai><m-pai>4s4s</m-pai>,</n-w> which is a <n-w><m-pai>3s</m-pai>-</n-w><m-pai>4s</m-pai> <i>shanpon</i> wait.
:::

Although <i>shanpon</i> waits are apparently “double” sided, they are treated separately in the five basic wait patterns. From a tile-efficiency perspective, <i>shanpon</i> is harder to win than <i>ryanmen</i> (or the real “double-sided” wait). Each tile type has only four copies in total. Compare <m-pai>5m6m</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>4m7m</m-pai> with <m-pai>5p0p1z1z</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>5p1z</m-pai>: in the first case there are 8 possible winning tiles outside; in the second, you already hold two of each target tile, so only 4 remain outside. That is the same count as a <i>kanchan</i> wait such as <m-pai>1p3p</m-pai> waiting on <n-w><m-pai>2p</m-pai>.</n-w> So, by raw tile count and ignoring table context (<i>bakyō</i>), double-sided (<i>ryanmen</i>) waits are best; <i>shanpon</i>, <i>kanchan</i>, and <i>penchan</i> waits follow; <i>tanki</i> waits are weakest. Double-sided and multi-sided waits are called **good shape** (<i>kōkei</i>), while the others are called **bad shape** (<i>gukei</i>).
