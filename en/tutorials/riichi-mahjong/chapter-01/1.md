# 1.1 Basic Mechanics

Riichi mahjong is a game with a strong luck component, yet over hundreds of games, differences in skill become very clear. This tutorial follows the standard Japanese mahjong rules that became more settled in the early 21st century, especially after online mahjong became widespread. Competitive rules represented by M.League (Ｍリーグ) differ from common rules in many details, and those differences will also be noted along the way.

Japanese mahjong terminology, like the game itself, was transmitted from China. The pronunciations of many Sino-Japanese terms are often close to older Beijing Mandarin forms. In addition, many terms have multiple synonymous names. I will try to mention as many as practical, but omissions are unavoidable, so please do not be surprised if you encounter variants in other materials.

## 1.1.1 Mahjong Tiles

## 1.1.2 How a Hand Proceeds

Let us start after the initial deal (*haipai*) is complete. At that point, each player has 13 tiles in hand, e.g.

<m-tehai>1m1m6m9m3p4p4p0p9p2s4s1z3z</m-tehai>

After that, players take turns in a draw-discard cycle:
- <strong>Draw</strong> (*tsumo*): first, draw the next tile from the live “wall” (*yama*[^yama]).
- <strong>Discard</strong> (*dahai*): then choose one tile from your hand and discard it.

[^yama]: Literally “hill; mountain.”

<m-tehai>1m1m6m9m3p4p4p0p9p2s4s1z3z　</m-tehai>draw <m-tehai>7z</m-tehai>

↓ discard<m-pai>9m</m-pai>

<m-tehai>1m1m6m3p4p4p0p9p2s4s1z3z7z</m-tehai>

Mahjong is a four-player game. Four players sit around a square table and take turns **counter-clockwise**. The player on your left is your *kamicha*; after they play, it is your turn. After your turn, it passes to your right-side *shimocha*, and the player across from you is called *toimen*. One full cycle of turns around the table is called one *jun*.

In each hand, one player is the **dealer** (*oya*). The other players are **non-dealers** (*ko*). In absolute seat terms, the dealer is the **east player** (*toncha*); moving counter-clockwise around the table, to their right is the **south player** (*nancha*), then the **west player** (*shācha*), and the **north player** (*peicha*)—regardless of the actual compass directions.

If any player wins (*hōra* or *agari*), that round ends immediately. Scores are settled, then all tiles are reshuffled for the next round. Rules about rounds will be discussed later; for now, let us first see how to win.

## 1.1.3 Winning and *Tenpai*

### 1.1.3.1 Winning Hand

Three identical tiles form a **triplet** (*kōtsu*):

<m-tehai>3p3p3p</m-tehai>, <m-tehai>6z6z6z</m-tehai>&nbsp;...

Three consecutive number tiles in the same suit form a **sequence** (*shuntsu*):

<m-tehai>3p4p5p</m-tehai>, <m-tehai>6s7s8s</m-tehai>&nbsp;...

Two identical tiles form a **pair** (*toitsu*):

<m-tehai>9m9m</m-tehai>, <m-tehai>1z1z</m-tehai>&nbsp;...

Triplets and sequences are collectively called **melds** (*mentsu*). The basic goal in mahjong is a 14-tile winning shape of <strong>4 melds + 1 pair</strong>, such as:

<m-tehai>4m5m6m4p4p4p6p7p8p2s3s4s8s8s</m-tehai>

This is often called **four melds and the eyes**. The “eyes” (*jantō*) is simply the pair inside a completed winning hand.

In Japanese mahjong, completing a winning shape is not enough by itself. You also need at least one valid *yaku* and must not violate *furiten* rules. Still, the core is the hand shape, and this tutorial will guide you through those additional requirements naturally.

### 1.1.3.2 *Tenpai*

As described above, each player starts with a 13-tile hand. On your turn, you draw a 14th tile and then make a discard to return your hand to 13 tiles. Eventually, a player will complete a 14-tile winning hand upon drawing a tile and declare a win. When your 13-tile hand is just one tile away from winning, this state is called ***tenpai***[^tenpai]:

[^tenpai]: Literally “listen to tiles.”

<m-tehai>5m6m4p4p4p6p7p8p2s3s4s8s8s</m-tehai>

This hand wins on either <m-pai>4m</m-pai> or <m-pai>7m</m-pai>. Drawing <m-pai>4m</m-pai> makes <m-pai>4m5m6m</m-pai>, while drawing <m-pai>7m</m-pai> makes <m-pai>5m6m7m</m-pai>. This is a ***ryanmen*** (two-sided) wait—where *ryan* translates to “two” and *men* to “sides.” There can also be 3-sided or even wider waits, for example:

<m-tehai>3m4m5m6m7m6p7p8p2s3s4s8s8s　</m-tehai>wait on<m-tehai>2m5m8m</m-tehai>

Wait classification matters later for *fu* calculation. This example is still categorized as *ryanmen*: if the winning tile is <m-pai>2m</m-pai>, parse it as <m-pai>3m4m　5m6m7m</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>2m5m</m-pai>; if it is <m-pai>8m</m-pai>, parse it as <m-pai>3m4m5m　6m7m</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>5m8m</m-pai>; if it is <m-pai>5m</m-pai>, either interpretation works.

### 1.1.3.3 Declaring a Win

Once you are in *tenpai*, you can win not only by drawing your winning tile yourself but also by winning on another player’s discard. In the case above, if you draw <m-pai>4m</m-pai> or <m-pai>7m</m-pai> yourself, you call **“*Tsumo*,”** reveal your full hand, and settle scores to end the hand. If another player discards either of these tiles, you call **“*Ron*”** to claim the win, followed by the same showdown and scoring procedure.

## 1.1.4 Wait Patterns

Within the standard four-melds-one-pair structure, besides *ryanmen* waits, there are four other wait (*machi*) patterns:

- If your hand has three complete melds and one pair, plus two same-suit tiles with one gap between them, that is a ***kanchan***[^kanchan] wait:
  <m-tehai>1s2s3s1p3p1m2m3m3m3m9m9m9m　</m-tehai>“wait on *kan*-<m-pai>2p</m-pai>”

  Can you spot which tiles are serving as the eyes here?
  
  A *ryanmen* wait has two winning tile types, while a *kanchan* wait has only one, so it is generally harder to win with.

[^kanchan]: Literally “inlay tile.”

- If the final incomplete shape is 1-2 or 8-9, that is a ***penchan***[^penchan] wait:

  <m-tehai>8s9s2p3p3p3p4p1z1z1z2z2z2z　</m-tehai>“wait on *pen*-<m-pai>7s</m-pai>”
  The tiles are sorted by rank. In dot tiles, this is sequence <m-pai>2p3p4p</m-pai> plus eyes <span style="white-space: nowrap;"><m-pai>3p3p</m-pai>.</span>

[^penchan]: Literally “side tiles.”

- If the hand already has three melds and the remainder is two pairs, waiting on either tile to make a triplet is a ***shanpon***[^shanpon] wait:

  <m-tehai>5p0p3s4s0s6m7m7m8m8m9m1z1z　</m-tehai>“wait on <m-pai>5p</m-pai>-<m-pai>1z</m-pai> *shanpon*”
  The character tiles are actually two sequences.

[^shanpon]: Literally “dual touch.”

- If four melds are complete and one lone tile remains, waiting for its match to form the pair is a ***tanki***[^tanki] wait:

  <m-tehai>1s3s3s3s4s4s4s6s6s6s8s8s8s　</m-tehai>“wait on <m-pai>1s</m-pai>-*tanki*”

  If you replace <m-pai>1s</m-pai> with <m-pai>2s</m-pai>, it becomes a 3-sided wait on <m-pai>1s2s4s</m-pai>. The block <m-pai>2s3s3s3s</m-pai> can be read either as pair <m-pai>3s3s</m-pai> + <m-pai>2s3s</m-pai> (wait on <m-pai>1s4s</m-pai> *ryanmen*) or as triplet <m-pai>3s3s3s</m-pai> + wait on <m-pai>2s</m-pai>-*tanki*.

[^tanki]: Literally “single-handedly on horseback.”

Although *shanpon* waits are apparently “double” sided, they are treated separately in the five basic wait patterns. From a tile-efficiency perspective, *shanpon* is harder to win than *ryanmen* (or the real “double-sided” wait). Each tile type has only four copies in total. Compare <m-pai>5m6m</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>4m7m</m-pai> with <m-pai>5p0p1z1z</m-pai> waiting on <m-pai>5p1z</m-pai>: in the first case there are 8 possible winning tiles outside; in the second, you already hold two of each target tile, so only 4 remain outside. That is the same count as a *kanchan* wait such as <m-pai>1p3p</m-pai> waiting on <span style="white-space: nowrap;"><m-pai>2p</m-pai>.</span> So, by raw tile count and ignoring table context (*bakyō*), double-sided (*ryanmen*) waits are best; *shanpon*, *kanchan*, and *penchan* waits follow; *tanki* waits are weakest. Double-sided and multi-sided waits are called **good shape** (*kōkei*), while the others are called **bad shape** (*gukei*).
